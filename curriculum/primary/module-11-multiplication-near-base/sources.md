## Where the finger trick comes from

The "hand multiplication" or "finger multiplication" trick for digits 6–9 is a folk method taught widely in schools across India and the world. It has no single named "first source" — it predates formal records. The algebra behind it is exactly the *Nikhilam* algebra (`(10 − a)(10 − b) = 10(10 − a − b) + ab`) — fingers-down = deficits, fingers-up = the deficit-product.

## Where the Sanskrit name comes from

- **Tirthaji, Bharati Krishna.** *Vedic Mathematics: Sixteen Mathematical Formulae from the Vedas* (1965), Motilal Banarsidass. — The Sanskrit name *Nikhilam Navataścaramaṁ Daśataḥ* ("all from 9 and the last from 10") was popularised by Tirthaji in this posthumously-published book.

## Honest history (for teachers, if asked)

Tirthaji claimed his sūtras came from an *Atharva-veda Pariśiṣṭa* (Vedic appendix text). No other scholar has produced this manuscript. Mainstream historians (Dani 1993, Plofker 2009) consider the system 20th-century synthesis rather than strictly ancient.

For Primary band the framing is simple: *"This trick has a Sanskrit name. The math is real. The history is older when you're older."*
