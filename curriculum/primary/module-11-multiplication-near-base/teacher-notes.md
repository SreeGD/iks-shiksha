# Teacher Notes — Module 11 Multiply-Near-Ten (Primary)

## Posture

This module gives Primary-band children a *single concrete win*: by Day 5 they can multiply any two digits from 6 to 9 using a finger trick. That's it. The full Nikhilam algorithm is reserved for Middle band. The finger trick IS Nikhilam at base 10 — but we don't make that connection explicit until Day 6, and even then it stays as "an older-grade name."

## Common things to handle

1. **A child can't yet recall all the deficit pairs.** That's fine — the module is the practice. Don't shame; bring them into more games.
2. **A child gets bored ("I already know my times tables!").** Give them the "speed challenge": all 16 problems (6×6 through 9×9) in under 30 seconds using the finger trick.
3. **A child asks "why does the finger trick work?"** Show the dot-pattern drawing on Day 4. If they want more, give them the *algebra hint*: "your fingers UP × fingers UP = the units. That's the deficit × deficit from older grades. You'll see it again."
4. **A child says "9 × 9 doesn't work on my fingers!"** It does — both hands have 1 finger down (deficit 1 for each). Fingers down = 2 → that's the tens (20). Fingers up = 4 left, 4 right → 4 × 4 = 16 → units. Total 20 + 16 = ... wait, the kids will need help. Show them the standard form: 9 × 9: deficits 1, 1; left 9 − 1 = 8; right 1 × 1 = 1 → **81**. The finger trick variation needs adapting for the smallest deficits.

## The finger trick (canonical form)

For `a × b` where both `a` and `b` are in 6–9:

1. Hold up both hands, palms toward you.
2. On the **left** hand, fold down `(a − 5)` fingers. Fingers DOWN on left = `a − 5`. Fingers UP on left = `10 − a`.
3. On the **right** hand, fold down `(b − 5)` fingers. Fingers DOWN on right = `b − 5`. Fingers UP on right = `10 − b`.
4. **Sum of fingers DOWN** on both hands = the TENS digit (×10).
5. **Product of fingers UP** on both hands = the UNITS digit.
6. **Tens + Units = answer.**

Example `7 × 8`:
- Left: fold down 2 fingers (7 − 5 = 2). 2 down, 3 up.
- Right: fold down 3 fingers (8 − 5 = 3). 3 down, 2 up.
- Down: 2 + 3 = 5. → **50**.
- Up: 3 × 2 = 6. → **6**.
- Answer: 50 + 6 = **56**. ✓

Example `6 × 7`:
- Left: 1 down, 4 up. Right: 2 down, 3 up.
- Down: 1 + 2 = 3. → 30.
- Up: 4 × 3 = 12. → 12.
- 30 + 12 = **42**. ✓

Example `9 × 9`:
- Left: 4 down, 1 up. Right: 4 down, 1 up.
- Down: 4 + 4 = 8. → 80.
- Up: 1 × 1 = 1. → 1.
- 80 + 1 = **81**. ✓

## The connection to Nikhilam (don't tell kids until Day 6)

- Fingers DOWN on left = `a − 5`. Fingers UP on left = `10 − a` = deficit of `a` from 10.
- Sum of fingers down = `(a − 5) + (b − 5) = a + b − 10`. Times 10 = `10a + 10b − 100`.
- Product of fingers up = `(10 − a)(10 − b)` = deficit × deficit.
- Sum: `10a + 10b − 100 + (10 − a)(10 − b)` = `10a + 10b − 100 + 100 − 10a − 10b + ab` = `ab`. ✓

So the finger trick **IS** Nikhilam — just with a different mnemonic that maps deficits to fingers-up. Children don't need this proof. The Middle module covers it.

## Safety

- No physical risk in this module.

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** Grade 5 children may be ready for 2-digit × 1-digit problems using the deficit idea (e.g. `8 × 12`). Or extend the finger trick to base 5 (multiplying 3–4 by 3–4 using a 1-hand variant).
- **Tier down:** Grade 3 children still building number sense need EXTRA game time on Days 1–2. Skip the finger trick on Day 3 and do another twins game; introduce the finger trick on Day 4 with the dot drawing as foundation.

## Project oversight

- Days 7–10: children make a poster or 4-panel comic.
- Materials: A4 sheet, crayons. Don't expect families to provide elaborate supplies.
- If a child can't draw, they can write the steps in big letters with arrows.

## Honest history — for parent or older-sibling questions

If a parent or older sibling asks: *"Is this real Vedic mathematics?"* — your answer is:
> "The finger trick is folk math taught all over the world; the Sanskrit name *Nikhilam* comes from a 1965 book by Tirthaji. The math is real and useful. We teach the technique here; the history we engage with in the older grades."
