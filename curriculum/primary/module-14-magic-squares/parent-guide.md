# Parent Guide — Module 14: Magic Squares (Primary)

**Your child's age:** Primary (Grades 3–5) · **Module length:** 10 sessions × ~30 min

## What your child is learning

**The puzzle:** put the numbers 1 to 9 in a 3×3 grid so every row, every column, and both diagonals all add up to the same number. That number turns out to be 15. We call this a **magic square**.

**Why "magic":** because every line in the square adds up to the same total — and that feels surprising. (It is not magic. It is careful arrangement.)

**Stories your child will hear:**
- The **Lo Shu turtle** — a Chinese story about an emperor who saw a 3×3 numbered pattern on a turtle's shell coming out of a river.
- The **Khajuraho stone** — a real carving on a temple wall in central India, showing a 4×4 magic square.

We treat the stories as stories. The carvings are real artefacts. The numbers are numbers.

## What to expect this fortnight

**Daily chant:** *"All to fifteen, all to fifteen!"* — your child will sing this at home. It helps the addition stick.

**Tile play:** your child uses paper tiles or tokens labelled 1–9 to try arrangements. Most days at school there is at least one hands-on session.

**Make-and-Keep (Day 9):** each child decorates their own 3×3 magic square page. This comes home on Day 10. Praise the effort, not just the result.

## How to help

**Helpful:**
- Ask: "Show me a row of your magic square that adds to 15." Let your child do the addition.
- Play "make 10": call out a single digit; your child says the partner that makes 10 (e.g. you say "7," they say "3"). 2 minutes a day is plenty.
- Read or listen with your child to the Lo Shu turtle story.

**Not helpful:**
- Showing your child the "answer" online. The discovery is the lesson.
- Pushing for 4×4 or bigger. That's Middle-band territory.
- Treating the magic square as magical or spiritual at this age. We keep it mathematical.

## What success looks like

By the end of the module, your child can:
- Add three single-digit numbers and check whether the sum is 15.
- Arrange the numbers 1 to 9 in a 3×3 to make a magic square (with help on early tries; independently by Day 9 for most children).
- Tell the Lo Shu turtle story in 3–4 sentences.
- Point to Madhya Pradesh on a map of India and say "the Khajuraho stone is there."
- Sing the "all to fifteen" chant.

## A note on the cultural framing

Magic squares have been used in some traditional contexts as protective or auspicious symbols. In this curriculum we engage them as mathematics. If your child mentions hearing about them in a religious context at home, that's wonderful — we simply approach them differently in the math class.
