# Teacher Notes — Module 14 Magic Squares (Primary)

## Posture

At Primary band, **the puzzle is the lesson**. Children discover that putting 1 to 9 into a 3×3 grid so every row, column, and diagonal sum to 15 *is hard* — and then discover that there's a satisfying pattern (5 in the centre, even numbers at the corners). They do not learn the formula and they do not construct anything bigger than 3×3.

The cultural content (Lo Shu turtle, Khajuraho stone) is a story-anchor for the math. Use story-time framing — not lecture.

## Common misconceptions

1. **"Any nine numbers will work."** No — we use exactly 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, each once. Reinforce by laying out the tiles.
2. **"There's only one row to check."** Make sure children count 3 rows + 3 columns + 2 diagonals = 8 sums to check. Eight stickers on a verified square.
3. **"The middle can be any number."** Quietly: 5 must be in the middle. We show this on Day 4 by hand (not algebra).
4. **"Magic squares are magic."** Reframe: "We call them magic squares because every line adds up to the same — that *feels* magic, but it's just careful arrangement."

## Differentiation hints

- **Tier up:** A child who has built the 3×3 confidently can try to make a 4×4 with 1–16 summing to 34. (They will fail without a method — that's fine. The struggle is the gift to Middle band.)
- **Tier down:** Stay on the chant and the "sums to 10" pair game. A child who can confidently say "8+2=10, 7+3=10, ..." is on track even if they haven't built the full square.

## Sensitivity notes

- Some children may have seen magic squares used in religious or astrological contexts at home (e.g. as *yantra*s). If a child mentions this, acknowledge warmly — "yes, magic squares are used in many ways. In this class we look at the numbers." Do not introduce ritual framing yourself.
- No timed competition between children. Praise effort and persistence, not speed.
- Children with arithmetic anxiety: pair them with a confident partner. The tile-laying is physical and helps.

## Behind the scenes (don't say to children)

The 3×3 magic square is *unique up to symmetry* — there are 8 arrangements (rotations + reflections) of the same underlying square. Children will often produce mirror images and think they're different. They are — and they aren't. At Primary band: praise both and don't worry about it.

## Project oversight (the Make-and-Keep)

- Day 9 is the make-and-decorate session. Most children will need help making sure their square is actually magic (i.e. that they didn't write 4 twice). Walk around and check.
- Day 10's "show and tell" is gentle. Each child speaks for 30 seconds about their square. Family-style audience.

## Safety

- Paper tiles only (no small parts that can be swallowed for younger Primary children).
- Coloured pencils only for decoration; no markers that bleed through.
