# Teacher Notes — Module 6 Moon Phases & Tithi (Primary)

## Posture for this module

Lead with **looking up**. Every class starts with "Did you see the Moon last night?" Children at this age are natural observers. The job here is to give them the language and the explanation, not to test them.

This is also a module where we can be playful — the Moon "visits" its house family, the torch-and-ball demo is just plain fun, festivals come into it. Lean into the playfulness. The Sanskrit terms (only two we insist on — *amāvasyā* and *pūrṇimā*) will stick because they sound interesting and they map to events children's families already celebrate.

## Common misconceptions to watch for

1. **"The Moon is following me when I walk."** It's a classic. Don't correct it on Day 1. By Day 4, when children have been observing it across many nights, they'll start to see it's in different places at different times. Let the discovery happen.
2. **"The Moon makes its own light."** A common belief. The torch-and-ball demo on Day 1 fixes this. If a child says it after Day 1, point at the ball when the torch is off — it goes dark. Reset.
3. **"The Moon disappears at amāvasyā."** It's still there. We just can't see it because the lit side is facing the Sun and the unlit side faces us. Worth saying explicitly on Day 2.
4. **"Eclipses happen at full moon AND new moon."** Solar eclipse → new moon (*amāvasyā*). Lunar eclipse → full moon (*pūrṇimā*). At Primary band, we don't go into "why not every month" — we just say "eclipses are rare and special."

## Pronunciation

Drill these aloud — children will love these two words:

- *amāvasyā* — "ah-MAH-vahs-yah" (4 syllables)
- *pūrṇimā* — "POOR-ni-mah" (3 syllables)

Sing them. Clap them. Make them stick.

## Safety — eclipses

The most important rule of the module:

> **NEVER LOOK AT THE SUN. NEVER LOOK AT A SOLAR ECLIPSE.**

Children at this age may have heard their parents prepare for an eclipse. Some families may have observed eclipse rituals. The classroom safety rule is unconditional and takes priority over any other interpretation. Drill it on Day 7. Make every child say it aloud.

Lunar eclipses are safe — children can look at them directly. Make this distinction clearly.

## Religious / secular framing

Several festivals discussed on Day 6 are religious holidays (Holī, Dīwālī, Karva Cauth, Janmāṣṭamī). At Primary band, the framing is **calendrical and cultural**: "These are festivals families in India celebrate, and they all happen on specific Moon-shapes." We don't go into the religious meanings unless a child volunteers one — then we acknowledge it ("yes, that's why some families celebrate it") and return to the calendar.

If a child's family does not celebrate any of these festivals, that's also normal — acknowledge it and ask if they have other festivals on a specific calendar date.

## Differentiation hints

- **Tier up (fast finishers):** Ask them to draw the 8-phase sequence (not just 4). Or, ask them to predict tonight's Moon shape from the calendar.
- **Tier down (scaffolding):** Reduce the Sanskrit names to just *pūrṇimā* (the easier one). Skip *amāvasyā* if a child is struggling.

## The Moon diary

If clear skies are unavailable (monsoon, light pollution, parent unavailable to take child outside), allow:
- Looking it up on an app at home with a parent.
- The teacher showing today's Moon shape from NASA's calendar at the start of class so children can copy.

The point is the **habit of asking** about the Moon each night. The data accuracy is secondary at Primary band.

## Day 3 — the folktale

Use the story as a teaching device, not as a claim about religious truth. Frame it as "an old story." Children love it. The astronomical content (the Moon takes ~27 days to come back to the same star) gets remembered better through the story than through a lecture.

## On Sanskrit terms beyond the two

Children may overhear *tithi, pakṣa, nakṣatra*, etc. from older siblings or parents. If they ask, acknowledge briefly:
- *tithi* — "a special Moon-day; we'll learn more next year."
- *pakṣa* — "the growing half or the shrinking half of the Moon's month."
- *nakṣatra* — "the Moon's house in the sky tonight."

Don't drill these. *Pūrṇimā* and *amāvasyā* are the two non-negotiables.

## Assessment posture

At Primary band, assessment is sticker-based (see `assessments/rubric.md`). No marks, no ranking. Children either showed up and participated, or they need a bit more time.
