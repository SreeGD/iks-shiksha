# Day 3 — The Moon's House Visits

**Module:** Moon Phases & Tithi (Primary) · **Time:** 30 min

## Goal

Children meet the idea that the Moon visits a different "house" (star pattern) every night — a folktale-style framing of the 27 *nakṣatra*s. The Sanskrit word *nakṣatra* is introduced softly without drilling.

## Materials

- A printed Moon and 27 paper "house" stars arranged in a circle on the floor (or on a wall poster)
- Drawing paper

## The flow

### Settle + recall (3 min)

Chant: *"am - mah - vahs - yah! poor - ni - mah!"* — three times.

Recall: *"Is the Moon growing or shrinking right now?"* (Refer to the day's actual sky if children have observed.)

### Story (12 min)

> **The Moon Visits Its House Family**
>
> Long, long ago, the Moon — whose name is *Soma* — had 27 sisters. Each sister lived in her own house, high up in the sky. Together they were the family of *nakṣatras* — the "star houses."
>
> Every night, the Moon visited ONE of his 27 sister houses. He never visited two on the same night. So one night he was in the house called *Aśvinī*. The next night, *Bharaṇī*. The next, *Kṛttikā*. And so on, all 27 sisters, one per night.
>
> After 27 nights, he had visited every sister. Then he started over — back to *Aśvinī*.
>
> *(Children love this. Walk a paper Moon around the 27 stars on the floor, naming a few of them as you go.)*
>
> Now — the Moon also changes shape every night, as we learned. Sometimes he is full (*pūrṇimā*), sometimes he disappears (*amāvasyā*). Some old stories say his sisters are happy when he is full, and worried when he disappears.
>
> But every month, he comes back to the same sister he visited a month ago. So if tonight the Moon is in the house of *Citrā*, in about a month, the Moon will visit *Citrā* again.
>
> That's why our months in India are named after the sisters. The month *Caitra* is named after *Citrā*. The month *Kārtika* is named after *Kṛttikā*. The Moon's sisters give the months their names.

### Activity (10 min)

Each child:
1. Draws the Moon in the middle of a paper.
2. Draws 4 little "house stars" around the Moon (we'll do 4, not 27).
3. Labels one of the houses with the name *Aśvinī* (or any nakṣatra name the teacher chooses).
4. Draws arrows showing the Moon's path from one house to the next.

This is a drawing exercise. Don't drill the names.

### Wrap-up (5 min)

- Show a few drawings.
- *"What did we learn? The Moon visits a new house every night. There are 27 houses in all. The Moon's sisters give our months their names."*
- **Homework:** *Look at the Moon tonight. What shape? Write the date.*

## Homework

- Continue the Moon Diary.

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** Try drawing all 27 houses in a ring around the Moon.
- **Tier down:** Just draw the Moon and one house. The story is the lesson — the drawing supports it.

## Teacher's quick notes

- The "27 sisters" framing is a teaching tool drawing on traditional stories (the Dakṣa's daughters narrative appears in several *Purāṇa*s, with the Moon as *Soma* / Candra). At Primary band, frame it as "an old story" — don't claim a single canonical source.
- The point: children learn that the Moon's position in the sky changes night by night, in a 27-night cycle (the *sidereal* month — different from the 30-day *synodic* month, but we don't need that distinction yet).
- If a child asks "why don't the houses come back to the same place every 30 nights?" — that's a great question, and you can tell them they'll learn about it in Middle school. (The answer: because Earth has moved around the Sun during the 27 days too.)
- Some children may know specific nakṣatras from their birth chart. That's fine — let them share, but don't dwell on astrology.
