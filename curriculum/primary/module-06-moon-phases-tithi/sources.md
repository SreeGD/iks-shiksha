# Sources — Module 6 Moon Phases & Tithi (Primary)

At Primary band, citations are not given to children. Teachers may use the following references for background:

## For the teacher

- **NCERT Science Class 6**, Chapter 17 ("Stars and the Solar System") — for the basic Sun–Moon–Earth geometry. Even though Class 6 is the upper boundary of Primary, the diagram and concept of phases are accessible.
- **NASA's published lunar phase calendars** — search "NASA Moon phases [current year]" — gives a reliable verification of the current month.
- **Any printed *pañcāṅga*** for the current year. Pick one (Drik Panchang, Mypanchang, or a regional almanac) and stick to it for the festivals discussion (Day 6).

## What we tell children about sources

Nothing. The framing is observational ("look at the Moon tonight") and story-led ("here is the story of the Moon visiting its house nakṣatras"). No citations appear in the children's workbook or homework.

## The folktale framing (Day 3)

The "Moon visits its house family each night" framing on Day 3 is **a teaching device**, not a folktale we are claiming to source. We tell children that the Indian tradition has 27 *nakṣatra*s (lunar mansions), and many old stories imagine these as 27 houses the Moon visits — one per night.

There ARE traditional stories about the Moon's relationship with the 27 nakṣatras (in some accounts, the 27 are daughters of Dakṣa, married to the Moon — *Soma* — who favours one over the others, leading to a curse and to the waxing/waning explanation). We tell this **as a story** without claiming a single canonical source — different versions appear across the *Purāṇa* literature. Honest framing: *"There are many old Indian stories about why the Moon changes shape. One of them goes like this..."*

## What we do not source at Primary

We do not cite the *Sūrya-siddhānta* to Primary children. We do not give Sanskrit verse numbers. We do not introduce *adhika māsa*, *yoga*, *karaṇa*, or detailed eclipse mechanics.

## On accuracy

Even though the framing is story-led, every astronomical claim made to children at Primary band is verifiable:

- "The Sun lights up half of the Moon" — true.
- "The Moon takes about 30 days to go around Earth" — close (29.5, which is fine for Primary).
- "There are 15 days between the new moon and the full moon" — close (14.75, fine).
- "Solar eclipses are dangerous to look at" — true.

If a child catches an inaccuracy, that's wonderful. Acknowledge it and correct gently.
