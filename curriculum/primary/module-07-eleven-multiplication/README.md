# Module 7 — The Eleven-Friend Trick

**Age band:** Primary · **Grades:** 3–5 · **Duration:** 10 sessions × 30 min

## What children will learn

1. The "Eleven-Friend" pattern: when any 2-digit number meets its "eleven friend," it grows by adding itself in the middle. (12 → 1 _ 2 → 1+2=3 → 132.)
2. The split-and-add trick on no-carry 2-digit numbers (digit-sum < 10).
3. A first peek at the carry case (a "spill-over" — on Day 6, just for awe).
4. A peek at 3-digit × 11 (Day 8) — they watch the teacher; they don't do it yet.
5. The Sanskrit name *Ekādhikena Pūrveṇa* introduced once, on Day 6, as "the older-kids' name for what we're doing."

## How this module sounds

- Lots of chanting. Drumbeat: split, add, drop.
- Story-based — every 2-digit number has an "eleven friend" who makes it bigger.
- No long worksheets. Children call out answers, draw, play.
- Sanskrit term *Ekādhikena Pūrveṇa* introduced once on Day 6.

## Materials

- A set of 2-digit number cards 10–98 (for the "meet your eleven friend" game)
- Whiteboards or small slates per child
- Chalk / markers
- A wall poster: "Split. Add. Drop."
- Counters (optional, for the spill-over demo on Day 6)
- Drawing paper

## Day-by-day

| Day | Title | What we do |
|-----|-------|------------|
| 1 | Meet Your Eleven-Friend | Story + first try with 12 × 11, 23 × 11 |
| 2 | Split, Add, Drop chant | Chant + 6 practice numbers |
| 3 | The Friend Game (no-carry) | Card-pair game |
| 4 | Speed Day | Pair quick-fire |
| 5 | What if the middle is too big? | Hint at the carry case |
| 6 | Meet the Sanskrit Name | *Ekādhikena Pūrveṇa* one-line intro |
| 7 | Make-a-Game Day | Design a "Make 11" game |
| 8 | A Peek at 3-digit ×11 | Teacher demo + awe |
| 9 | Project Day | Build the game |
| 10 | Game Showcase | Play each other's games |

## Project

Children design their own "Eleven Friend" card or board game in pairs. They play each other's on Day 10.

See `assessments/project-brief.md`.

## Sources

The ×11 trick is a mental-math pedagogical technique taught in Indian schools. The Sanskrit name *Ekādhikena Pūrveṇa* comes from Tirthaji's *Vedic Mathematics* (1965). For older grades' deeper treatment (including the algebraic proof and the carry case), see the Middle-band version of this module.
