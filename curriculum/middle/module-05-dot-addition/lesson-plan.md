## Pacing notes

- Day 1 establishes a baseline speed (each student times their own standard-algorithm addition of a 4×4-digit sum). Day 5 retests the SAME problem set with the dot method; the visible speedup is the module's motivation.
- Days 2–4 are the core. Compressed schedule: combine Days 2+3 into one period if needed.
- Day 6 ("Why it works") can be pushed to Day 7+ if the class is enjoying the drills.
- Project (Days 8–10) is shorter than Panchabhuta's because the artefact options are more constrained.

## Daily warm-up (recurring)

Every class starts with **1 minute of "make-10 fluency"**: teacher calls out single digits; students call back the partner that makes 10. (8 → 2, 6 → 4, 9 → 1, etc.) This is the foundational skill.

## What students leave the module with

- Fluency at scanning a column of numbers and finding 10-pairs.
- Confidence adding 4-digit, 5-addend columns in their head or with paper.
- Awareness that "Indian Math" is not a single ancient block — it includes 20th-century pedagogical innovations like this one.
- One practice artefact (their project) they can share with younger students.
