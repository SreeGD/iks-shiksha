# Day 6 — Why It Works

**Module:** Bindu Paddhati · **Band:** Middle · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

By the end of this lesson, students will explain in their own words why the dot method gives the same answer as the standard algorithm, citing place value and the fact that a "dot" represents a 10 carried.

## Materials

- Whiteboard.
- The 16-Sūtra list (`Microsoft Word - sutras1.pdf`) — printed and visible.
- Worksheet: "explain to a friend" prompt + space.

## Lesson flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

- Make-10 round.
- Hand back formative. Discuss any common error pattern from the quiz briefly.

### Core (20 min) — The mathematical reasoning

1. **The core claim** (write boxed on the board):

   > **A dot in a column represents "10 has been used up." Since 10 in any column equals 1 in the next column, the dot count is the carry.**

2. **Walk through a simple example to prove it**:

   ```
   Column: 8, 7, 2, 6, 4

   Direct addition: 8+7+2+6+4 = 27.
     Units = 7. Carry = 2.

   Dot method:
     Start 0. +8 = 8. +7 = 15 → dot · on 7, total = 5.
     +2 = 7. +6 = 13 → dot · on 6, total = 3. +4 = 7.
     Units = 7. Dots = 2. Carry = 2.

   Same answer.
   ```

3. **Why? Because each dot is precisely "I just crossed 10."**
   - Crossing 10 once = 1 carry.
   - Crossing 10 twice = 2 carry.
   - The leftover is just whatever remains in the running total.

4. **Connection to Tirthaji's 16 sūtras.** Display the list:
   - Sūtra #7: **Sankalana Vyavakalanābhyām** — "by addition and by subtraction."
   - Sūtra #8: **Pūraṇāpūraṇābhyāṁ** — "by completing or non-completing."
   - The dot method is *not* itself one of these sūtras — it's a 20th-century pedagogical technique that uses the *spirit* of these sūtras (looking for completions to a base).
   - Be honest: "There is no ancient Sanskrit verse that says 'use the dot method.' What we have is the *idea* of completion that classical Indian math used, and a modern teaching tool that makes it concrete."

### Activity (15 min) — Explain to a friend

- Worksheet prompt:
  > Imagine your Grade 4 cousin asks: *"Why does that dot trick work? Isn't it just magic?"* Write 4–6 sentences explaining why it's NOT magic. Use the words *carry*, *place value*, and *10*.

- 10 min individual writing. 5 min pair-sharing: read your paragraph to your partner; they tell you one part that's clear and one part that's unclear.

### Wrap-up (5 min)

- Read aloud one strong paragraph (with permission).
- Preview Day 7: *"Tomorrow — using the same 'completion to 10' trick for mental addition of 2-digit numbers near a base."*

## Student-facing content

> **Why the dot method works**
>
> A column of digits is in some place-value column — units, tens, hundreds. When the running total reaches 10, that's literally **one** of the next place value. So the dot is just a *visible* carry.
>
> The 16 sūtras of Vedic mathematics (Tirthaji, 1965) include *Sankalana Vyavakalanābhyām* ("by addition and by subtraction") and *Pūraṇāpūraṇābhyāṁ* ("by completing or non-completing") — both use the *idea* of looking for completions to a base (like 10). The dot method makes this idea concrete and visible.
>
> **It is not magic. It is good notation.**

## Homework

- Find ONE example in your daily life where you naturally "complete to 10" without thinking. (E.g. adding tip to a bill — round to 10. Counting change. Adding minutes to a clock.) Write 2 sentences describing it.

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** Write a 1-paragraph explanation suitable for a Grade 11 student. Include the connection to base-10 representation and to the carry algorithm.
- **Tier down:** Skip the writing; just verbalise the explanation to your partner.

## Teacher notes

- The honest "not in any sūtra" framing matters. Some students (or parents) believe the dot method is "ancient Vedic mathematics" — clarify gently. The achievement here is good *teaching*, which is also a real achievement.
- The most common error in student paragraphs: confusing the dot with "skip counting." Reinforce that the dot represents *the carry*, not "I'm skipping a number."
