# Teacher Notes — Module 5 Dot Addition (Middle)

## Posture

This module is about **speed and pattern recognition**, not about memorising a Sanskrit name. The win is when a student instinctively scans a column of numbers and groups them into 10s without conscious thought. The Sanskrit term *Bindu Paddhati* is the cultural anchor; the *technique* is what they take home.

Honesty about provenance: the dot-column method described in this module is a 20th-century pedagogical technique. It draws on the same instinct as Tirthaji's sūtras *Sankalana Vyavakalanābhyām* and *Pūraṇāpūraṇābhyāṁ*, but it is not itself one of the 16 sūtras. Don't oversell it as "ancient Vedic math." At Senior band students unpack this distinction in detail; at Middle band just don't claim what isn't true.

## Common misconceptions

1. **"This method is slower because I have to write more (the dots)."** True for some students initially. Practice fixes it — by Day 5 the dot method should be at least 50% faster on multi-addend columns.
2. **"I only need to look for exact 10s — 9+1, 8+2, etc."** Also accept 9+2 (= 11) — put down 1, carry one dot. The point is to *group efficiently*, not to find exact 10s only.
3. **"The dot is the same as the carry digit."** Yes — each dot represents one "1" carried. If you have 3 dots in a column, you carry 3. Reinforce this on Day 6.
4. **"This is just regrouping in disguise."** Partly true — and that's fine. It's a *visual* technique for regrouping that exploits the fact that scanning beats serial addition.

## Differentiation hints

- **Tier up:** Skip dots entirely and do the running total in your head. Faster students should be doing fully mental column addition by Day 7.
- **Tier down:** Use coloured pens — different colour for each 10-group identified in a column. The colour scaffolds the "scan and group" action.

## Differential pacing

- A class with strong arithmetic baseline (most students adding 4-digit + 4-digit in <30 sec) can compress the module to 6–7 days by combining Days 1+2 and Days 4+5.
- A class with shaky baseline addition should NOT skip Day 2. The "make-10" fluency is the prerequisite for everything else.

## Sensitivity notes

- Some students are anxious about timed math. Be clear that the timed activities are **personal baselines** — students race themselves, not each other. Public leaderboards are *not* used.
- Avoid the framing "the West has standard algorithm, India has the fast method" — both methods are taught in Indian schools, both are *useful*, and the standard algorithm is itself partly of Indian origin via Brahmagupta. Module 6 (future, "Astronomy") will touch on this.

## Project oversight

- Three project formats (60-sec teaching video, printed workbook, comparative-speed data project) each have different production challenges.
- Video projects: check before Day 8 that the pair has access to a phone with a camera AND a quiet place to record. Otherwise redirect.
- Speed-test data projects: students collect data from 5–10 family members or schoolmates. They time both methods. Confirm by Day 9 that they actually have the data — otherwise the project collapses on Day 10.

## Citation discipline

Citations for this module are PDF references (e.g. *Vedic Mathematics Batch1.pdf, §5.4*), not scripture references. Students should cite the PDF name and section in their projects. This is the standard for math modules in this curriculum.

## If you only have 6 days

- Day 1 (hook + baseline)
- Day 2 (combined: making tens + single column with dots)
- Day 3 (multiple columns)
- Day 4 (mid-module check + mental patterns combined)
- Day 5 (one project session)
- Day 6 (final assessment, no project presentation)
