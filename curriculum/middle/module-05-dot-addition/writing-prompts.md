# Writing Prompts — Module 5 Dot Addition (Middle)

Six prompts. Useful as exit tickets, homework reflections, or project warm-ups.

## Reflection prompts

**1. (Day 1, end of class, 3 min)** What does it feel like to add a column of numbers under time pressure? Describe what your mind does — do you talk through the addition, see numbers, count on fingers?

**2. (Day 7, end of class, 5 min)** Yesterday you had to write "why the dot method works." Today you used it for mental addition. Has your *feeling* about math speed changed in the last week? Be honest — even if the answer is "no."

## Explanatory prompts

**3. (Day 3 or Day 6, homework, ~120 words)** Explain the dot method to a Grade 4 student who has never seen it. No more than 6 sentences. Use one specific example.

**4. (Day 7, homework, ~100 words)** Explain the *Sankalana Vyavakalanābhyām* idea (add to the round number, take away the difference) in your own words. Use one example with addition and one with subtraction.

## Argumentative prompt

**5. (Day 6 or homework, 1 paragraph)** Some people claim the dot method is "ancient Vedic mathematics." Others say it's a 20th-century pedagogical technique inspired by Vedic mathematics. Pick a position and defend it in 5–7 sentences. Use what you know about the 16 sūtras.

## Creative prompt

**6. (Day 4 or Day 5, optional, 1 page)** Write a short script (50–150 words) in which a frustrated student and a confident dot-method user have a conversation. The frustrated student raises one good objection to the dot method. The confident user gives a clear, honest answer. (No "and then they magically agreed" endings — let the disagreement be real.)

---

## Prompt-to-band notes

- For **Primary** band, prompts 1, 3, and 6 work as drawings or oral responses.
- For **Senior** band, prompt 5 deserves a full essay treatment with citations from `sources.md`.

## How to give feedback

- Prompts 1, 2: acknowledge with one sentence — no correction.
- Prompts 3, 4: respond with "the part that landed" and "the part that confused me."
- Prompt 5: use the rubric — does the writing acknowledge the other side, give a specific example, reach a clear position?
- Prompt 6: no grading. Read the best ones aloud (with permission).
