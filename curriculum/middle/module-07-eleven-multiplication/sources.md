# Sources — Module 7 Eleven Multiplication (Middle)

The **vidya-karana RAG** does not cover Vedic-math sources, so this module draws directly from a small set of published references plus the SutraGanita PDF corpus (`/Users/sree/Projects/SutraGanita/content/`).

## Primary source

**Tirthaji, Bharati Krishna.** *Vedic Mathematics: Sixteen Simple Mathematical Formulae from the Vedas* (1965, edited posthumously by V. S. Agrawala). Delhi: Motilal Banarsidass.

- **Sūtra 1 — *Ekādhikena Pūrveṇa*** ("by one more than the previous"). Tirthaji applies this sūtra mainly to recurring-decimal division (e.g. 1/19 = 0.052631…); the ×11 multiplication trick is presented as a related corollary. The version we teach in this module is the standard pedagogical form found in every Vedic-math secondary reference.
- **Sub-sūtra — *Anurūpyeṇa*** ("proportionally"). Used to scale the ×11 pattern to ×12, ×13, …, ×19.
- The book was completed in manuscript form between 1911 and 1918 (per the editor's foreword) and published in 1965, five years after Tirthaji's death (1960).

> Cite as: Tirthaji (1965), *Vedic Mathematics*, sūtra 1.

## Supporting sources (SutraGanita corpus)

- **Microsoft Word - sutras1.pdf** — single-page reference listing all 16 main sūtras and 13 sub-sūtras with English glosses. Used on Day 8 ("Tirthaji's Sūtra System in Context").
- **Vedic_Maths.pdf** (ISKCON Desire Tree) — workbook-style. Contains practice sets for the ×11 trick. Used for Day 4–5 drills.
- **Vedic Mathematics Batch1.pdf** (Vedic Cultural Center, Sammamish WA) — secondary reference with worked examples of the ×11 method, including the carry case.

## Historical critique (used on Day 8)

- **Dani, S. G.** "Myths and Reality: On Vedic Mathematics." *Frontline* (1993), reprinted in *Resonance* vol. 6 no. 10, October 2001, pp. 53–65. Argues that no manuscript of the *Atharva-veda Pariśiṣṭa* containing these 16 sūtras has been produced, and that the techniques are best understood as a 20th-century mental-arithmetic synthesis.
- **Plofker, Kim.** *Mathematics in India* (2009), Princeton University Press. Standard reference on the documented history of pre-modern Indian mathematics; covers (e.g.) Brahmagupta, Bhāskara II, the Kerala school. Tirthaji's sūtras are mentioned in passing as a 20th-century claim outside the documented manuscript tradition.

## What we DON'T do

We do not claim the ×11 trick "comes from the Vedas." We say it is presented in Tirthaji's 1965 book under a Sanskrit sūtra name; whether the sūtra itself is verifiably Vedic is the question raised honestly on Day 8.
