# Teacher Notes — Module 7 Eleven Multiplication (Middle)

## Posture

This module is about **mental fluency plus algebraic understanding**. The two reinforce each other: students who only memorise the trick freeze on the carry case; students who only prove the algebra never get the speed. We want both.

The Sanskrit name *Ekādhikena Pūrveṇa* is the cultural anchor. The *trick* is what students take home for life.

**Honesty about provenance.** The ×11 technique is widely taught as "Vedic mathematics." The honest framing for this module:

- Tirthaji (1965) names the trick using a Sanskrit sūtra (*Ekādhikena Pūrveṇa*).
- The book was published posthumously (Tirthaji died 1960; book 1965).
- Tirthaji claims the sūtras come from an *Atharva-veda Pariśiṣṭa*, but no other scholar has produced this manuscript.
- The trick works mathematically; the "Vedic" attribution is academically disputed (Dani 1993, Plofker 2009).

At Middle band we state this briefly on Day 8 and let students sit with the ambiguity. Don't moralise either way.

## Common misconceptions

1. **"The middle digit IS the sum, even when it's two digits."** Students write 73 × 11 as 7-10-3 (a "5-digit number" with a 10 in the middle). The carry step fixes this; reinforce Day 2.

2. **"Add 1 to the answer, not to the leftmost digit."** When the digit-sum carries, the +1 goes into the **first digit**, not into the running answer. Watch for "73 × 11 = 8(10)3" being written as 813.

3. **"It's just multiplication, so why a special name?"** True observation. Don't dismiss it. Reframe: the name labels a *pattern*; the pattern lets you do this mentally instead of writing it out. Names matter when they make things fast.

4. **"The trick is Vedic."** Most students arrive believing this. Day 8 is where we honour their belief but also lay out the academic critique. No shaming.

5. **"3-digit × 11 is too hard to do in my head."** Almost no one can on Day 4. By Day 5–6 most students can. Practice gap fills slowly.

6. **"*Anurūpyeṇa* is *too* hard."** It is harder than ×11 — that's structural. Some classes may benefit from skipping ×17/×19 and staying with ×12/×13.

## Differentiation hints

- **Tier up:** Push to 4-digit × 11, then 3-digit × 12 to 19. The brave will love the cascading-carry challenge in 4-digit × 11.
- **Tier down:** Stay on 2-digit × 11 (no carry) until Day 3. Some students need the foundation before any extension.

## Differential pacing

- A class with strong arithmetic baseline can compress to 7 days by combining Days 4+5 (3-digit + speed day) and Days 7+8 (limits + history).
- A class with shaky multiplication baseline should NOT skip Day 3 (the proof) — the algebra is what makes the trick reliable rather than memorised-and-forgotten.

## Sensitivity notes

- Some students will have parents or coaching backgrounds where the trick is taught as ancient sacred knowledge. Be respectful. Frame the academic critique as "what the scholarly consensus says," not "what your parents got wrong."
- Avoid civilisational comparisons ("Indians had this before Westerners"). The technique is its own thing; what makes it Indian is Tirthaji's synthesis and the Sanskrit naming, not any race for priority.
- The competitive-mental-math kids will be ahead from Day 1. Use them as peer-coaches; reframe their advantage as "you'll explain the WHY this week."

## Project oversight

- Three formats (video, workbook, proof poster) each have different challenges:
  - **Video:** check before Day 9 that the pair has phone access and a quiet recording space.
  - **Workbook:** simple to start, easy to abandon. Set a 15-problem minimum.
  - **Proof poster:** recommended for pairs who struggled on Day 3 — making the algebra public forces internalisation.

- The project here is intentionally smaller than Module 5's. Don't let it bloat.

## Citation discipline

- Citations come from the SutraGanita PDF corpus, plus Tirthaji (1965) and Dani (1993/2001).
- Students should cite specific PDFs or books in their projects. The standard is `<source>, <section/page>`.

## If you only have 6 days

- Day 1: Hook + baseline
- Day 2: Carry case
- Day 3: Why it works (algebra)
- Day 4: Combined — 3-digit × 11 + *Anurūpyeṇa* intro
- Day 5: Combined — limits + Tirthaji context
- Day 6: Final assessment, no project

Skip the project entirely if needed; keep the algebra.

## What I wish someone had told me first time teaching this

- The cascading-carry case (99 × 11 = 1089) is dramatic. Use it on Day 2 and milk it.
- Students who can do the trick fast but can't explain why are NOT mastered yet. Push them to verbalise.
- The Day 8 discussion can feel deflating if you're not careful. Frame Tirthaji as a real intellectual achievement BEFORE introducing Dani's critique. "Both can be true."
