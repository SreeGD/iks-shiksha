- What shape was it?
- Was it growing or shrinking?
- What time did you see it?

This module is built around that observation.

---

# The Moon doesn't change. Our view changes.

- The Sun lights up half of the Moon — always.
- As the Moon orbits Earth, we see different fractions of that lit half.
- One full cycle = ~29.5 days.

This is the **synodic month**.

---

# The Eight Phases

| 1 | new moon | invisible |
| 2 | waxing crescent | thin sliver, sets after Sun |
| 3 | first quarter | half-disc, lit on the right |
| 4 | waxing gibbous | almost full |
| 5 | full moon | full disc, rises at sunset |
| 6 | waning gibbous | full but shrinking |
| 7 | last quarter | half-disc, lit on the left |
| 8 | waning crescent | thin sliver, rises before Sun |

---

# Two Pakṣas

| Sanskrit | English | Moon is |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *śukla pakṣa* | bright fortnight | waxing (growing) |
| *kṛṣṇa pakṣa* | dark fortnight | waning (shrinking) |

Anchor days:

- *amāvasyā* — new moon
- *pūrṇimā* — full moon

---

# What is a Tithi?

> **One tithi = the time during which the Moon gains 12° of elongation on the Sun.**

- 360° / 30 = 12° per tithi.
- 30 tithis per synodic month (15 per pakṣa).
- Average ~23h 37m — **NOT** exactly 24 hours.

---

# The 15 Tithi Names

*pratipadā · dvitīyā · tṛtīyā · caturthī · pañcamī · ṣaṣṭhī · saptamī · aṣṭamī · navamī · daśamī · ekādaśī · dvādaśī · trayodaśī · caturdaśī · pūrṇimā* (or *amāvasyā*)

Sanskrit ordinals 1–14, then the anchor day.

# 12 Lunar Months

*Caitra, Vaiśākha, Jyeṣṭha, Āṣāḍha, Śrāvaṇa, Bhādrapada, Āśvina, Kārtika, Mārgaśīrṣa, Pauṣa, Māgha, Phālguna.*

Each named after the *nakṣatra* in which its *pūrṇimā* falls.

---

# Pañcāṅga — Five Limbs

| Limb | What it tracks |
|------|----------------|
| *tithi* | Moon's elongation from Sun |
| *vāra* | day of the week |
| *nakṣatra* | Moon's position against stars |
| *yoga* | Sun + Moon longitudes |
| *karaṇa* | half-tithi |

A snapshot of the sky for any given day.

---

# Eclipses: solar

Sun — Moon — Earth, in that order.

Happens on *amāvasyā*.

Moon's shadow falls on Earth.

⚠️ **NEVER look directly. Use ISO 12312-2 certified eclipse glasses.**

---

# Eclipses: lunar

Sun — Earth — Moon, in that order.

Happens on *pūrṇimā*.

Earth's shadow falls on Moon.

Safe to view directly. Moon may turn coppery-red.

---

# Why not every month?

The Moon's orbit is tilted ~5° from Earth's orbit around the Sun.

So usually the Moon is above or below the Sun-Earth line at amāvasyā / pūrṇimā.

Eclipses happen only near the orbital **nodes** (where the two orbits cross).

About 2–5 solar + 0–3 lunar eclipses per year.

---

# Adhika Māsa — the Extra Month

12 lunar months × 29.53 days = **354.36 days**

Solar year = **365.24 days**

The lunar calendar drifts behind by ~11 days a year.

Solution: insert an *adhika māsa* every ~32.5 months. This makes the Indian calendar **lunisolar**.

---

# Cross-cultural lunar calendars

| Calendar | Type | Intercalation |
|----------|------|---------------|
| Indian | Lunisolar | *adhika māsa* |
| Islamic (Hijri) | Pure lunar | None |
| Chinese | Lunisolar | Leap month |
| Hebrew | Lunisolar | 7 leap months in 19 years |

The Indian, Chinese, and Hebrew systems converge on lunisolar intercalation.

# Modern astronomy vs. pañcāṅga

Both describe the same Moon. Different formats:

- A modern ephemeris (e.g. NASA Horizons) lists positions in degrees, dates, magnitudes.
- A *pañcāṅga* lists tithi, nakṣatra, yoga, karaṇa, vāra.

Both useful for different audiences.

# The Moon Diary (10 nights)

Look up every night for 10 nights.

Sketch the Moon's shape. Note the tithi. Note the cloud cover.

If cloudy: look up the predicted phase from a published source.

The habit of *noticing the Moon* is the deeper learning.
