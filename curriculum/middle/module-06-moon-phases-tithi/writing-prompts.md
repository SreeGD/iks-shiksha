# Writing Prompts — Module 6 Moon Phases & Tithi (Middle)

Eight prompts in mixed modes. Assign as in-class quick-writes (10 min), homework (one paragraph), or as journal entries woven across the module. Each prompt names the lesson day where it fits naturally.

## Reflection prompts

**1. (Day 1, 5 min)** What did you think made the Moon change shape, before today? In 3–5 sentences, describe what you used to believe, and what changed your mind in class today. (If you already knew the geometry, write about a younger sibling or friend's belief instead.)

**2. (Day 7, 10 min)** Stand outside for two minutes one evening (or open the window). Look up. Then list the Moon's position, its shape, and the tithi. In 4 sentences, describe whether the actual sky matched the *pañcāṅga* prediction. (If it didn't, what might explain the difference?)

## Explanatory prompts (in your own words)

**3. (Day 2 or 3, homework, ~150 words)** Explain to a younger sibling (Grade 4) why a tithi is not the same as a day. Don't use the word "elongation" — find your own way to say it.

**4. (Day 6, in-class, 10 min)** Imagine you are explaining the *pañcāṅga* to a friend from another country who has never seen one. In 5 sentences, describe what the five limbs are and why having all five gives you a more complete picture than any one limb alone.

## Argumentative prompt

**5. (Day 6 or Day 7, homework, 1 paragraph)** The modern Gregorian calendar ignores the Moon entirely — months are 30 or 31 days regardless of the Moon's actual phase. The Indian *pañcāṅga* tracks the Moon precisely but needs an *adhika māsa* every ~32 months to stay aligned with the seasons. Pick a side and defend it in 5–7 sentences: which approach is better for *modern civic life*, and which for *cultural/agricultural life*? Acknowledge the other side's strongest point before disagreeing.

## Creative prompt

**6. (Day 5 or Day 8, can be project — 1 page)** Write a short scene (50–150 words) in which the Sun, Earth, and Moon have a conversation about why an eclipse is happening today. Each character gets at least one line. End with one of them explaining why this doesn't happen every month.

## Cross-disciplinary prompt

**7. (Day 6 or homework, ~120 words)** The Indian *pañcāṅga* and a modern astronomy ephemeris (e.g. NASA Horizons) do similar work — both list astronomical facts for each day. Pick another field — geography, music, finance, sports — and describe a similar pairing: one traditional system and one modern system that do the same job in different formats. (Example: traditional Indian musicology's *rāga* system and modern Western music theory both organise pitch — different formats, same domain.)

## Stretch prompt

**8. (Optional, advanced students, Day 10 onwards, ~250 words)** The *Sūrya-siddhānta* (Indian, c. 5th–10th c. CE) and the *Almagest* of Ptolemy (Greek, 2nd c. CE) both describe lunar motion in detail and both use geometric models with epicycles. They were developed independently. In a single page, argue for OR against the claim: *"Two cultures arriving at similar models of the same sky is evidence that the sky's behaviour, not the cultures' biases, drives astronomy."* Use one specific example to support your argument.

---

## Prompt-to-band notes

- All prompts are usable at Middle band as written.
- For **Primary** band, prompts 1, 2, 3, and 6 can be adapted (shorter, drawing-friendly).
- For **Senior** band, prompts 4, 5, 7, and 8 are essay-quality without modification; add a citation requirement.

## How to give feedback

- Short prompts (1, 2): respond with one sentence of acknowledgement, not corrections. The point is observation.
- Explanatory (3, 4): respond with "the part that landed" and "the part still unclear." Don't correct grammar unless severely impeding meaning.
- Argumentative (5, 8): use the rubric — does the writing acknowledge the other side, give a specific example, and reach a clear position?
- Creative (6): no grading. Read aloud the best ones if you have permission.
