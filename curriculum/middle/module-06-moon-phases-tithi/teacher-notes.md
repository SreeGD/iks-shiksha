# Teacher Notes — Module 6 Moon Phases & Tithi (Middle)

## Posture for this module

Lead with what students can see — the Moon, tonight, in the sky. The *tithi* system is a sophisticated organisation of those observations, refined by Indian astronomers over many centuries. It is not folklore. It is also not modern astronomy. It is a calendrical scheme that does some things modern astronomy does not bother with (a unit smaller than a day, tied to the actual position of the Moon) and skips some things modern astronomy demands (a fixed integer number of days per month).

Treat the *Sūrya-siddhānta* as a serious astronomical text — one of several in the world's history of astronomy — not as a mythological document. If a student asks "is this religious?" — the honest answer is that the *pañcāṅga* is used both for religious timing (festival dates) and for civil/agricultural timing. We use it here as a calendrical and astronomical instrument; the religious uses are real and respected but not our focus.

## Common misconceptions to watch for

1. **"A tithi is one day."** A tithi is 1/30 of a synodic month, not 1/30 of a calendar day. Average length ~23.6 hours, but can range from ~21.5 to ~26 hours because the Moon's motion is non-uniform. A given tithi may "skip" a sunrise (*kṣaya*) or span two sunrises (*vṛddhi*).
2. **"The new moon means no Moon."** The Moon is still there during *amāvasyā*. It's just between the Sun and Earth from our perspective, so the illuminated half faces away from us.
3. **"Phases happen because of Earth's shadow."** A very common confusion. Phases happen because the Sun lights up only half of the Moon at any time, and we see different fractions of that lit half as the Moon orbits Earth. Earth's shadow causes only lunar *eclipses*, not phases.
4. **"Eclipses happen every new moon and full moon."** They would, if the Moon's orbit were in the same plane as Earth's orbit around the Sun. The Moon's orbit is tilted ~5°, so eclipses happen only when the Moon crosses through this plane near new or full moon. About 2–5 solar eclipses and 0–3 lunar eclipses per year.
5. **"All Indian calendars start on the same date."** No — *amānta* (month ends at new moon, used in much of South and Western India) and *pūrṇimānta* (month ends at full moon, used in much of North India) traditions number the months differently. The same tithi can belong to different months under different conventions.
6. **"The śukla pakṣa is the 'good' fortnight."** Both pakṣas are simply astronomical halves. Festivals and rituals are tied to both — *amāvasyā* (kṛṣṇa pakṣa) is auspicious for ancestor rites; *pūrṇimā* (end of śukla pakṣa) is auspicious for other observances.

## Safety — eclipse viewing

This is the most important safety note in the module:

- **NEVER look directly at a solar eclipse**, even partial. Looking even briefly without proper protection can cause permanent retinal damage. Ordinary sunglasses do not work. Smoked glass does not work. Welder's glass below shade #14 does not work.
- **Safe methods**: ISO 12312-2 certified eclipse glasses, pinhole projection (project the Sun's image onto a sheet of paper through a small hole), or telescope projection (never look through the telescope — project the image).
- **Lunar eclipses are safe to watch directly.** They are dim — sometimes the Moon turns coppery-red ("blood moon") because the only light reaching it is sunlight bent through Earth's atmosphere.

State this safety rule at least twice in Day 7. Make every student write it in their workbook.

## On Sanskrit pronunciation

Drill these terms aloud in week 1; students will refer to them throughout the module:

- *tithi* — "TI-thi" (short *i* both times)
- *pakṣa* — "PUK-sha" (the *ṣ* is a retroflex sh)
- *śukla* — "SHU-kla"
- *kṛṣṇa* — "KRISH-na" (the *ṛ* is a vowel)
- *amāvasyā* — "ah-MAH-vahs-yah"
- *pūrṇimā* — "POOR-ni-mah"
- *pañcāṅga* — "PUN-chahn-ga"
- *nakṣatra* — "NUCK-shu-tra"

## Religious / secular framing

Some families observe fasts (*vrata*) on specific tithis — *ekādaśī* (11th), *caturdaśī* (14th), *pradoṣa* (13th evening), etc. A student may volunteer this. Acknowledge it briefly — *"yes, that's a traditional practice tied to this tithi"* — and return to the astronomical content. Do not lead with the religious dimension.

If a student asks why eclipses are "considered inauspicious" — answer historically: most cultures across the world treated eclipses as significant events, often inauspicious, because they were rare and unpredicted in pre-scientific contexts. Today we understand the geometry and can predict eclipses years in advance; the astronomical fact is decoupled from the cultural interpretation.

## Differentiation hints

- **Tier up (fast finishers):** Ask them to compute the tithi at a specific time on a specific date using the elongation formula (`tithi = floor((Moon_longitude - Sun_longitude) mod 360 / 12) + 1`). They will need an astronomy app for the longitudes; this is genuinely how the modern *pañcāṅga* is computed.
- **Tier down (scaffolding):** Reduce the first lesson to "the Moon changes shape because the Sun lights it up from different angles" — defer the precise geometry to Day 7's diagram. Memorisation of all 15 tithi names can wait until Day 5.

## Sensitivity notes

- Some students may come from families who consult a *pañcāṅga* daily for muhūrta (auspicious timing). Honour that without privileging it. The framing is "this is an astronomical instrument that has these other uses in the tradition."
- Avoid implying that Indian astronomy "anticipated" or "was ahead of" Western astronomy. The histories are parallel; both made remarkable observations; both made errors. Treat the *Sūrya-siddhānta* as an astronomical text on its merits.

## If a clear sky is unavailable for the observation diary

This is a real risk during monsoon or in light-polluted cities. Allow students to use:
- A NASA-published lunar phase image for the date.
- An astronomy app (Stellarium, SkyView, etc.) showing the Moon's phase for the date.
- A reliable online phase calendar.

State up front that the diary's purpose is the *habit of looking up*. If they can't see the Moon, they should still attempt — note "cloudy, could not see" and the expected phase from a published source.
