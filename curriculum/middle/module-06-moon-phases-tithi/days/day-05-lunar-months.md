# Day 5 — Lunar Months and the Festival Calendar

**Module:** Moon Phases & Tithi · **Band:** Middle · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

Students will name the 12 lunar months in order, explain how each month is named (after the *nakṣatra* of its *pūrṇimā*), and link at least three festivals to their tithi-and-month positions.

## Materials

- Board with a 12-row table ready
- A printed list of major festivals across the 12 lunar months (one per pair)
- Notebooks

## Vocabulary introduced today

- *māsa* (IAST: māsa; lit. "month") — a lunar month, ~29.53 days.
- *nakṣatra* (IAST: nakṣatra; lit. "lunar mansion") — one of 27 segments of the ecliptic, each ~13°20' wide. (Full coverage in a later module — today's mention is just for naming.)
- *adhika māsa* (IAST: adhika-māsa; lit. "extra month") — an intercalary 13th month inserted approximately every 32.5 months.

## Lesson flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

- Daily prompt: *"Did you see the Moon last night? What tithi?"* — 2 students share.
- Quick recall using yesterday's tithi calendar: *"What month did we build the calendar for?"* (May, June, etc.) *"In the Indian lunar calendar, what month is that?"* (Answer: it depends — let's find out.)

### Core (20 min)

1. **The 12 lunar months.** Write the list on the board:

   | # | Lunar month (IAST) | Roughly corresponds to | Named after |
   |---|---------------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
   | 1 | *Caitra* | March–April | nakṣatra *Citrā* |
   | 2 | *Vaiśākha* | April–May | *Viśākhā* |
   | 3 | *Jyeṣṭha* | May–June | *Jyeṣṭhā* |
   | 4 | *Āṣāḍha* | June–July | *Pūrva-* / *Uttara-āṣāḍhā* |
   | 5 | *Śrāvaṇa* | July–August | *Śravaṇa* |
   | 6 | *Bhādrapada* | August–September | *Pūrva-* / *Uttara-bhādrapadā* |
   | 7 | *Āśvina* | September–October | *Aśvinī* |
   | 8 | *Kārtika* | October–November | *Kṛttikā* |
   | 9 | *Mārgaśīrṣa* | November–December | *Mṛgaśīrṣa* |
   | 10 | *Pauṣa* | December–January | *Puṣya* |
   | 11 | *Māgha* | January–February | *Maghā* |
   | 12 | *Phālguna* | February–March | *Phālgunī* (Pūrva / Uttara) |

2. **Why these names? The naming convention.** A lunar month is named after the *nakṣatra* in which the *pūrṇimā* (full moon) of that month falls. The Moon at full moon is opposite the Sun, so it sits in a specific star background — the nakṣatra. The lunar month inherits its name from that nakṣatra.

3. **Two month-counting conventions.** This is a real difference and worth noting:

   - *Amānta* — the lunar month ends at *amāvasyā*. Common in South and Western India (Maharashtra, Karnataka, Andhra, Tamil Nadu, Gujarat).
   - *Pūrṇimānta* — the lunar month ends at *pūrṇimā*. Common in North India (Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, North Indian Hindi belt).

   So the same fortnight can be called by different month names under the two conventions! For example, the *kṛṣṇa pakṣa* before Janmāṣṭamī is "*Śrāvaṇa kṛṣṇa pakṣa*" under *pūrṇimānta* and "*Bhādrapada kṛṣṇa pakṣa*" under *amānta*. Both are correct — they are counting from different anchor points.

4. **Linking festivals to tithis — the festival table.** Write 6 examples:

   | Festival | Lunar month + pakṣa + tithi | What it celebrates / who observes |
   |----------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------|
   | *Ugādi / Gudi Pādvā* | *Caitra* śukla pratipadā | Lunar new year (in much of South / West India) |
   | *Rāma Navamī* | *Caitra* śukla navamī | Birth of Rāma |
   | *Akṣaya Tṛtīyā* | *Vaiśākha* śukla tṛtīyā | Auspicious for beginnings |
   | *Guru Pūrṇimā* | *Āṣāḍha* pūrṇimā | Honouring teachers |
   | *Janmāṣṭamī* | *Bhādrapada* (or Śrāvaṇa, depending) kṛṣṇa aṣṭamī | Birth of Kṛṣṇa |
   | *Dīpāvalī / Dīwālī* | *Kārtika* amāvasyā | Festival of lights |
   | *Mahā-śivarātri* | *Phālguna* (or Māgha) kṛṣṇa caturdaśī | Great night of Śiva |
   | *Holī* | *Phālguna* pūrṇimā | Festival of colours |

   Every festival here has a precise tithi-and-month coordinate. That's the whole point of the *pañcāṅga* system.

5. **The intercalation puzzle — *adhika māsa*.** 12 lunar months × 29.53 days = 354.36 days. The solar year is 365.24 days. So the lunar calendar drifts behind by ~11 days a year. After 3 years that's 33 days — about a whole month. To re-align, the Indian system inserts an *adhika māsa* ("extra month") roughly every 32.5 months. This is what makes the Indian calendar **lunisolar** rather than purely lunar. (More on this in the cross-cultural lesson, Day 9.)

### Activity (15 min) — Festival mapping + formative quiz

**Part A (8 min) — pair work.** Each pair gets the printed festival list (about 12 festivals). Their job:

1. Mark which lunar month each festival falls in.
2. Identify the tithi and pakṣa.
3. Find ONE festival that no one in the pair had heard of before. Write its month + pakṣa + tithi.

**Part B (7 min) — formative quiz**, see `quizzes/formative.md` Part B. Collect and mark within 24 hours.

### Wrap-up (5 min)

- One student names a festival their family celebrates; the class names the tithi+pakṣa+month. Two rounds.
- **Project decision deadline:** by the end of today, every student has a project partner (or has confirmed individual work) and a tentative topic. Sticky note on the board.

## Student-facing content

> **There are 12 lunar months. Each is named after the *nakṣatra* in which its *pūrṇimā* falls.**
>
> *Caitra, Vaiśākha, Jyeṣṭha, Āṣāḍha, Śrāvaṇa, Bhādrapada, Āśvina, Kārtika, Mārgaśīrṣa, Pauṣa, Māgha, Phālguna.*
>
> Two conventions: months end at *amāvasyā* (*amānta*) or at *pūrṇimā* (*pūrṇimānta*). Different regions, different conventions — same astronomy.
>
> Every Indian festival sits at a precise (month + pakṣa + tithi) coordinate. The *pañcāṅga* tells you exactly when.

## Teacher notes

- The *amānta* / *pūrṇimānta* distinction confuses everyone the first time. It's OK to leave students slightly fuzzy on it — the key insight is that conventions vary regionally, and "the calendar" isn't one fixed thing.
- Some students will know a festival under a regional name (e.g. *Gudi Pādvā* vs. *Ugādi* — both *Caitra* śukla pratipadā). Honour both.
- The "12 months are named after nakṣatras" point doesn't need full nakṣatra coverage today — that comes in a later module. Today we just say *what* the naming convention is.
