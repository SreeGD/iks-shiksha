# Day 8 — Project Session 1: Plan and First Artefact

**Module:** Moon Phases & Tithi · **Band:** Middle · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

Students will produce a one-paragraph project plan with element (lunar topic), format, audience, and citation; and complete a first-draft artefact (poster sketch / video storyboard / slide outline).

## Materials

- Project supplies for whatever format students chose: poster paper, markers, A4 sheets, laptops, art supplies
- Printed copy of the rubric (`assessments/rubric.md`) — one per pair
- The class *pañcāṅga* (for reference)
- Days 1–7 student notebooks (their own diaries + worksheets)

## Lesson flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

- Daily prompt: *"Did you see the Moon last night? What tithi?"* — 2 students share.
- Hand each pair a printed rubric. *"Read through the rubric — 4 minutes. Bring questions to me."*

### Core (5 min)

Take questions on the rubric. The most common questions:

- *"Do we need to quote a verse?"* — No. A paraphrase of the *Sūrya-siddhānta* (e.g. "the Sūrya-siddhānta defines a tithi as 12° of elongation") is enough. A NASA reference is also acceptable. The rubric cares that your source is real and named.
- *"Can we do TWO topics?"* — One main topic. You may compare to a second briefly.
- *"What if our diary is missing some nights (clouds)?"* — Note "cloudy, predicted [shape]" for those nights. The diary still counts.

### Activity (30 min) — Build

Pairs work on their projects. The teacher rotates and checks each pair has:

1. **Chosen topic** — one of:
   - Moon phases (geometry and the 10-night diary visualised)
   - Eclipses (why, when, safety)
   - Tithi calendar (the one built on Day 4 + an explanation)
   - Cross-cultural lunar calendars (preview of Day 9)
   - Lunar months and festivals
   - Adhika māsa (intercalary month)

2. **Chosen format** — one of:
   - Poster (A2 or larger)
   - Slide deck (5–8 slides, projected)
   - 90-second video
   - 1-page printed story / explainer
   - Live demonstration with the torch and ball
   - Infographic / data visualisation

3. **One source named** — *Sūrya-siddhānta* (paraphrased), NCERT Physics XI, NASA's phase calendar, the class *pañcāṅga*, etc. **Real sources only.**

4. **By end of class:** a one-paragraph plan + a first-draft artefact (a poster sketch, video storyboard, slide outline).

The teacher intervenes if:
- A pair is still debating topic at minute 15 — give them 5 minutes, then assign.
- A pair's source is invented or unverifiable — flag it; help them swap.
- A pair has split unequally — re-balance the workload before they leave.

### Wrap-up (5 min)

- Each pair submits their plan paragraph + first-draft artefact (phone photo accepted).
- **Tomorrow:** finish the build. The cross-cultural calendar lesson runs in parallel — 15 min content, then 30 min build time. Be ready.

## Student-facing content

> **Today is build day.**
>
> By the end of this class, your pair (or you, if solo) should have:
> - A one-paragraph plan in writing.
> - A first-draft artefact — poster sketch, slide outline, video storyboard.
> - Your source named, on the artefact.
>
> Tomorrow you refine. Day 10 you present.

## Homework

- Continue the Moon diary (you have 2 nights left).
- Finish at home what you couldn't finish in class. Don't *start* something new — refine.

## Differentiation

- **One tier up:** Add a second citation. Two sources is a stronger project — for instance, paraphrase the *Sūrya-siddhānta* AND cite NASA's phase calendar.
- **One tier down:** Reduce scope. Pick ONE phenomenon (phases, eclipses, or the calendar) and explain it in one paragraph + one diagram. That's a complete project.

## Teacher notes

- Pairs that are still "researching" by minute 20 are stuck. Push them to start sketching/drafting even if incomplete. The artefact concretises the thinking.
- Phone-photo submissions are fine. Photograph the draft poster against a wall; submit by email or via shared drive.
- A common failure mode: pairs over-design the visual and under-write the explanation. Push toward writing the **3-minute script** by end of class.

## Citation(s) used in this lesson

- The rubric (`assessments/rubric.md`) is the primary reference for what "good" looks like.
- Source-quality reminder: ONLY real sources. No invented citations.
