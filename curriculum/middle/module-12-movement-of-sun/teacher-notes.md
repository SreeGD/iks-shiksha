# Teacher Notes — Module 12 Movement of the Sun (Middle)

## Posture for this module

Lead with what students can observe directly: the Sun rises in the east-ish, sets in the west-ish, but the exact spot on the horizon shifts north and south through the year. The Indian terminology (*uttarāyaṇa, dakṣiṇāyana, rāśi, saṁkrānti*) names what you can see, then provides a framework for calculation. Modern astronomy explains the underlying mechanism — Earth's axial tilt and orbital motion. Both descriptions are about the same sky. The module trains the habit of holding the observational vocabulary and the mechanistic explanation together.

If a student asks "but isn't astrology nonsense?" — distinguish between *jyotiṣa* as **observational astronomy** (which is what this module is about: tracking solar position, predicting *saṁkrānti* dates, computing the calendar) and *jyotiṣa* as **horoscope prediction** (claims about how planetary positions affect individual lives — outside the science scope of this module). Indian astronomical texts contain both; we teach the first.

## Common misconceptions to watch for

1. **"The Sun goes around the Earth."** Geocentric appearance is real; we observe the Sun's *apparent* motion. Earth orbits the Sun (heliocentric reality). The two descriptions are tied by relative motion. Don't shame the "Sun moves" framing — it's the framing the Sūrya-siddhānta and Aristotle and Ptolemy all used. Indian astronomers from Āryabhaṭa onward did consider Earth's rotation; the heliocentric model was finally pinned down in Europe in the 16th–17th century.

2. **"India has summer in winter because it's in the south."** No. The Northern Hemisphere has its summer when the *north* end of Earth's axis tilts toward the Sun, which happens in June. In June it's hot across most of India. India's summer = June, not December.

3. **"Makara Saṁkrānti is the winter solstice."** Close, but not exactly. In the *sāyana* (tropical) frame, the winter solstice is ~21 December. In the *nirayana* (sidereal) frame, the Sun enters *Makara* around 14 January. The two used to coincide ~1700 years ago, but precession has spread them apart by ~24°. Day 7 unpacks this. **Don't fudge it** — the apparent contradiction is the whole interest of the topic.

4. **"Sāyana is right and nirayana is wrong" (or vice versa).** Neither is "right." They are two different reference frames. *Sāyana* (tropical) is fixed to the equinoxes — useful for the seasons. *Nirayana* (sidereal) is fixed to the stars — useful for long-term astronomical observation. Modern astronomy uses *sāyana* for civil calendars and *nirayana* for star catalogues; Indian traditional almanacs (*pañcāṅgas*) use *nirayana* for festival dates.

5. **"The Sun is in a constellation matching my horoscope sign on my birthday."** It is in the *sāyana* sign matching popular Western horoscope columns (those use the tropical zodiac), but the Sun is NOT visually in front of that constellation on that date — because of precession. This is a common eye-opener; let students discover it on Day 7.

6. **"Each rāśi is 30 days long."** Roughly, but not exactly. Because Earth's orbit is elliptical, the Sun's apparent angular speed varies — so some *rāśi* transits last ~29 days, others ~31. The mean is 30.44 days (365.25 / 12).

## On Sanskrit pronunciation

Drill these terms aloud across the module — they are not familiar to most students:

- *uttarāyaṇa* — "oo-ttuh-RAH-yuh-nuh" (the ṇ is retroflex; tongue tip touches the back of the palate)
- *dakṣiṇāyana* — "duck-SHI-nah-yuh-nuh" (*kṣ* is "ksh" or "ksh-ya")
- *saṁkrānti* — "sung-KRAHN-tee" (the ṁ is a nasal hum, mostly anusvāra)
- *viṣuva* — "VI-shoo-vuh"
- *rāśi* — "RAH-shi" (ś is the "sh" of "ship")
- *sāyana* / *nirayana* — "SAH-yuh-nuh" / "ni-ruh-YUH-nuh"
- *ayanāṁśa* — "uh-yuh-NAHM-shuh"
- *krānti-vṛtta* — "KRAHN-ti-VRIT-tah"

## Why this module is hard to teach

The *sāyana* / *nirayana* distinction (Day 7) is the genuine intellectual challenge. Many adults — even teachers — quietly carry the wrong idea that *Makara Saṁkrānti* IS the winter solstice. Day 7 is engineered to surface and resolve this. Plan for one student per class to push back hard ("then the calendar must be wrong!"). It isn't wrong; it answers a different question. The traditional calendar tracks the Sun's position **against the fixed stars**; the modern civil calendar tracks the Sun's position **against the equinoxes**. Both are internally consistent; they diverge because the equinoxes precess against the stars.

If the class is stuck, draw this diagram: a circle (the ecliptic), a fixed star marker (the *aśvinī* nakshatra), and a moving arrow marker (the equinox). 2000 years ago they overlapped. The arrow has slid back ~24°. That's *ayanāṁśa*.

## Differentiation hints

- **Tier up (fast finishers):** Compute their own birthday's Sun position — both *sāyana* and *nirayana* — using a website like *Stellarium* (free desktop app) or *time-and-date.com*. Compare to their newspaper horoscope sign.
- **Tier down (scaffolding):** Reduce the 12 *rāśi* names to 6 (the four solstice/equinox markers plus two others). Drill these in pairs across Days 5 and 6. The remaining six can come in the project work.

## Sensitivity notes

- Astrology is a touchy subject. Some families read horoscopes and *pañcāṅgas* daily; others reject astrology as superstition. The classroom frame is neither. We teach the **astronomy** behind the calendar (what does "the Sun is in Makara" mean as an observable fact about the sky) without taking a position on whether the placement determines personality or fortune.
- *Makara Saṁkrānti* is celebrated as Pongal, Bihu, Lohri, Maghi, and Uttarayan across India. Acknowledge this if students bring it up — it's a calendar festival rooted in the astronomy we're studying. Don't turn the lesson into a festival lesson; do honour the cultural connection.

## Safety — Sun viewing

**Never** allow students to look directly at the Sun. Standard solar-eclipse-grade filters are the only safe direct-view tool. For this module, all observation is **shadow-based**: the gnomon's shadow on a flat surface tells you where the Sun is without you needing to look up. Treat any "I'll just peek for a second" as a hard NO.

## Scientific-religious framing

*Uttarāyaṇa* and *dakṣiṇāyana* are mentioned in the Mahābhārata (Bhīṣma chooses to die during *uttarāyaṇa*, considered auspicious) and elsewhere in the Sanskrit corpus. We acknowledge this in passing — it shows that the astronomy was part of the lived culture — but we **do not teach the religious significance** in class. The astronomy is what's testable; the religious framing belongs to a different subject. Be ready to redirect: "we'll learn what *uttarāyaṇa* means astronomically here; if you want to discuss why it was considered auspicious, that's a Sanskrit-literature conversation, not a science one."
