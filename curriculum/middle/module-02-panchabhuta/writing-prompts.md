# Writing Prompts — Module 2 Panchabhūta (Middle)

Eight prompts in mixed modes. Assign as in-class quick-writes (10 min), homework (one paragraph), or as journal entries woven across the module. Each prompt names the lesson day where it fits naturally.

## Reflection prompts

**1. (Day 1, 5 min)** Pick the element that surprised you most today. In 3–5 sentences, describe a moment from your morning when this element was around you and you didn't notice it. Now that you've noticed, what changes?

**2. (Day 7, 10 min)** Sit somewhere quiet for two minutes (you can do this in class or at home before writing). Then list the five elements and rank them by which one is *most present* and which is *least present* where you sat. Write 4 sentences explaining your top and bottom ranking.

## Explanatory prompts (in your own words)

**3. (Day 2 or 3, homework, ~150 words)** Explain to a younger sibling (Grade 4) why earth has all five qualities but space has only one. Don't use the word "tanmātra" — find your own way to say it.

**4. (Day 6, in-class, 10 min)** Imagine you are a chemistry teacher being asked about *pañcabhūta* by a curious Grade 10 student. In 5 sentences, answer the question: *"Is the pañcabhūta framework outdated?"* Be fair to both frameworks.

## Argumentative prompt

**5. (Day 6 or Day 7, homework, 1 paragraph)** A modern science textbook says fire is "not an element" — it is hot gas with light from incandescent particles. The *pañcabhūta* tradition says *tejas* IS an element. Pick a side and defend it in 5–7 sentences. Acknowledge the other side's strongest point before disagreeing.

## Creative prompt

**6. (Day 5 or Day 8, can be project — 1 page)** Write a short scene (50–150 words) in which the five elements have a conversation about who is the most important. Each element gets at least one line. End with one element pointing out something the others have missed.

## Cross-disciplinary prompt

**7. (Day 6 or homework, ~120 words)** Pick a topic from another subject — geography, biology, music, sport, anything. Show how the *pañcabhūta* lens would describe one feature of that topic that the standard subject vocabulary misses. (Example: in music, *ākāśa* is the silence between two notes; in football, *vāyu* is the wind that bends a free kick.)

## Stretch prompt

**8. (Optional, advanced students, Day 10 onwards, ~250 words)** The Greek philosopher Empedocles (5th century BCE) proposed FOUR elements: earth, water, fire, air. There is no equivalent of *ākāśa* in his scheme. Aristotle later added a "fifth essence" — but it was the substance of the heavens, not of space-in-general. In a single page, argue for OR against the claim: *"Including ākāśa makes the Indian framework more powerful than the Greek one."* Use one specific example to support your argument.

---

## Prompt-to-band notes

- All prompts are usable at Middle band as written.
- For **Primary** band, prompts 1, 2, 3, and 6 can be adapted (shorter, drawing-friendly).
- For **Senior** band, prompts 4, 5, 7, and 8 are essay-quality without modification; add a citation requirement.

## How to give feedback

- Short prompts (1, 2): respond with one sentence of acknowledgement, not corrections. The point is observation.
- Explanatory (3, 4): respond with "the part that landed" and "the part still unclear." Don't correct grammar unless severely impeding meaning.
- Argumentative (5, 8): use the rubric — does the writing acknowledge the other side, give a specific example, and reach a clear position?
- Creative (6): no grading. Read aloud the best ones if you have permission.
