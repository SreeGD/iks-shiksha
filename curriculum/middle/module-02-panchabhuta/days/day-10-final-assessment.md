# Day 10 — Final Assessment + Project Showcase

**Module:** Panchabhūta · **Band:** Middle · **Time:** 45 min (potentially extend to 60 if presentations need it)

## Learning objective

By the end of this lesson, students will demonstrate mastery of the module's learning objectives through a summative quiz and a 3-minute project presentation.

## Materials

- Printed summative quiz (`quizzes/summative.md`) — one per student.
- Stopwatch.
- Rubric copy (printed) for each pair, with teacher marking column.
- A "reflection slip" — one per student.

## Lesson flow

### Warm-up (3 min)

- No daily prompt today.
- Brief: "Two parts today — a quiz first, then your presentations. Quiz is 20 minutes, individual. Presentations after."

### Part 1: Summative Quiz (20 min)

- Distribute `quizzes/summative.md`. Closed-book. No discussion.
- Quiz covers: element names (Sanskrit + English), creation sequence, quality-count rule, tanmātra-to-sense mapping, one comparison question on pañcabhūta vs. modern science, one open-response on element imbalance.
- Collect quizzes at 20-min mark.

### Part 2: Project Presentations (20 min)

- 3 min presentation + 1 min Q&A per pair.
- With ~8–10 pairs in a typical class, you'll fit 5 pairs in the remaining 20 min. **Reserve the next class period for the remaining presentations.** (Note this honestly — Day 10 alone is rarely enough for 10+ pairs.)
- During each presentation:
  - One teacher marks the rubric in real-time.
  - One audience member is assigned to ask one specific question.
  - Audience completes a "1 thing I learned, 1 question I have" slip per presentation.

### Wrap-up (2 min)

- Thank the class.
- Closing question (oral, voluntary): *"What's one thing you'll keep noticing after this module ends?"*
- Reflection slip due: students answer the three reflection questions from `assessments/project-brief.md`.

## Student-facing content

> **Today: quiz + presentations**
>
> - 20 minutes for the summative quiz — closed book.
> - 3-minute presentation per pair, then 1 minute of audience questions.
>
> When you're in the audience, fill out one "I learned / I wonder" slip per presentation. These go to the presenters and help them know what landed.
>
> **Reflection (due at the end of class):**
>
> 1. What did you set out to learn or make, and what did you actually end up with?
> 2. What is the most surprising thing you discovered while making this?
> 3. If you had three more days, what would you do differently?

## Homework

- None. The module is complete. (Begin the next module's prerequisite reading if your sequence flows directly into another.)

## Differentiation

- **One tier up:** A student who finishes the quiz early can write a 1-paragraph "what would Module 2 look like for a class of Grade 11 students?" — useful input for the Senior-band pack.
- **One tier down:** Allow the quiz to be open-notebook for a student with documented learning needs — your call.

## Teacher notes

- Block out 30 minutes after class for marking the quiz while presentations are fresh.
- If presentations spill into Day 11, that's fine — protect that time. Don't compress presentations to fit; you lose the assessment validity.
- Mark the rubric in real-time, but write detailed comments only after each presentation finishes — the comment is what students keep.
