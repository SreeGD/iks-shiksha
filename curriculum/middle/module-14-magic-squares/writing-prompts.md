# Writing Prompts — Module 14 Magic Squares (Middle)

Six prompts. Useful as exit tickets, homework reflections, or project warm-ups.

## Reflection prompts

**1. (Day 1, end of class, 3 min)** When you tried to build the 3×3 magic square by trial and error, what was going through your head? Did you have a strategy, or were you just guessing? Be specific.

**2. (Day 7, end of class, 5 min)** You spent three days using Nārāyaṇa's *turagagati* algorithm before learning who Nārāyaṇa was. Did learning about him change how you feel about the method? (Either answer — yes, no, somewhere in between — is fine, but explain it.)

## Explanatory prompts

**3. (Day 4 or homework, ~120 words)** Explain the *turagagati* method to a Grade 5 student who has never seen it. Use no more than 7 sentences. Include one specific example.

**4. (Day 6 or homework, ~120 words)** Explain *why* the Khajuraho 4×4 square is called "panel-magic." Use the word **constraint** in your answer.

## Argumentative prompt

**5. (Day 8 or homework, 1 paragraph)** Some sources call magic squares "Indian magic" or "Chinese magic" depending on the culture. Others insist that magic squares appeared independently in many cultures, and no single culture "owns" them. Pick a position and defend it in 5–7 sentences. Use what you know about Lo Shu, Khajuraho, Nārāyaṇa, and Dürer.

## Creative prompt

**6. (Day 7 or Day 8, optional, 1 page)** Write a short dialogue (50–150 words) between Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita (1356 CE) and Albrecht Dürer (1514 CE), meeting across time. They show each other their magic squares. Let them disagree on something — maybe about *why* they care about these patterns. Don't have them magically agree.

---

## Prompt-to-band notes

- For **Primary** band, prompts 1, 3, and 6 work as drawings or oral responses.
- For **Senior** band, prompt 5 deserves a full essay treatment with sources cited from `sources.md`.

## How to give feedback

- Prompts 1, 2: acknowledge with one sentence — no correction.
- Prompts 3, 4: respond with "the part that landed" and "the part that confused me."
- Prompt 5: use the rubric — does the writing acknowledge the other side, give a specific example, reach a clear position?
- Prompt 6: no grading. Read the best ones aloud (with permission).
