# Sources — Module 14 Magic Squares (Middle)

## Primary source

**Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita, *Gaṇita-kaumudī* (1356 CE) — Book 14: *Bhadragaṇita*** ("Mathematics of Auspicious Squares").

- The *Gaṇita-kaumudī* is a Sanskrit mathematical compendium in 14 books (*vyavahāras*). Book 14, *Bhadragaṇita*, is dedicated to magic squares and is the most extensive pre-modern Indian treatment of the topic.
- Nārāyaṇa presents construction methods for: (a) odd-order squares via a stair-step / *turagagati* placement, (b) even-order squares of orders divisible by 4 via a complement-pair method, and (c) "singly even" orders (6, 10, 14...) via a modified method.
- For Middle band we use only the odd-order method, since it is fully accessible with a single rule.

> Cite as: Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita, *Gaṇita-kaumudī*, Book 14 (*Bhadragaṇita*), 1356 CE.

## Secondary scholarly source

**Kim Plofker, *Mathematics in India* (Princeton University Press, 2009).**

- Chapter 8 ("The School of Mādhava") and surrounding context cover 14th-century Indian mathematics. Magic squares are discussed in the context of Nārāyaṇa's combinatorial work.
- Used as the bridge between Sanskrit primary source and a modern teacher-friendly summary.

> Cite as: Plofker (2009), *Mathematics in India*.

## Additional historical context (teacher reference)

- **Hayashi, T.** Multiple papers in *Historia Mathematica* and *Indian Journal of History of Science* on the chronology and content of pre-modern Indian mathematics, including Nārāyaṇa's work on combinatorics and magic squares.
- **Sarma, S. R.** Work on Indian astronomical and mathematical instruments; sometimes discusses *yantra* diagrams that include magic squares.
- These are background reading for the teacher; not cited to students directly.

## Material artefacts

- **Khajuraho 4×4 magic square inscription.** Found at the Khajuraho temple complex (Madhya Pradesh, India); dated *circa* 10th–11th c. CE — the exact date is debated. The square is known as a "panel-magic" square (also called "pan-diagonal" or "most-perfect"): rows, columns, broken diagonals, *and* every 2×2 panel sum to the same constant.
- **Lo Shu (China).** Earliest secure attestation in the *Da Dai Liji* (Han dynasty, ~5th–1st c. BCE). Legend attributes the discovery to Emperor Yu (~2000 BCE) seeing a turtle whose shell carried the pattern — that legend is *not* taken as historical evidence here.
- **Albrecht Dürer, *Melencolia I* (1514).** The print's upper-right corner contains a 4×4 magic square where the bottom-middle two cells encode the date "1514."

## What to tell students about the citation chain

The construction methods we teach are real and ancient: Nārāyaṇa's *turagagati* algorithm is exactly the same algorithm modern textbooks now call the *Siamese method* or the *de la Loubère method* — but Nārāyaṇa described it nearly three centuries before de la Loubère reported it from his 1687 embassy to Siam. We will cite Nārāyaṇa as the historical first systematic exposition we have in writing. We will *not* claim he was the only inventor, nor the first ever — there are earlier hints in Indian, Chinese, and Arabic sources. The honest framing is: "Nārāyaṇa gave us the most complete pre-modern systematic treatment we have."
