# Parent Guide — Module 14: Magic Squares (*Bhadragaṇita*)

**Your child's age:** Middle (Grades 6–8) · **Module length:** 10 sessions × 45 min

## What your child is learning

**A magic square** is a square grid of distinct numbers where every row, every column, and both main diagonals add up to the same total — the *magic constant*. The smallest non-trivial example uses the numbers 1 to 9 in a 3×3 grid; every line sums to 15.

**The construction method.** Your child will learn an algorithm — Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita's *turagagati* ("horse-step") method — for building a magic square of any odd order (3×3, 5×5, 7×7, ...). The same method appears in modern textbooks under the names *Siamese method* or *de la Loubère method*; Nārāyaṇa documented it nearly 300 years before the European description.

**The history.** Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita wrote his *Gaṇita-kaumudī* in 1356 CE. Book 14 of that work, *Bhadragaṇita*, is the most systematic pre-modern treatment of magic squares we have in writing. Your child will also meet the Chinese *Lo Shu* square (~5th c. BCE earliest evidence), the Khajuraho temple inscription (Madhya Pradesh, ~10th–11th c. CE), and Albrecht Dürer's 1514 European square — to see that the topic appears across cultures.

## A note on the cultural framing

Magic squares have been used in some traditional contexts as *yantra*s — protective or contemplative diagrams. In this curriculum we study them as **mathematics**: as a problem in combinatorics. We acknowledge the broader cultural uses without engaging in any ritual framing. The mathematics is real; the cultural breadth is real; the curriculum keeps a clear scholarly stance.

## What to expect this fortnight

**Daily warm-up:** 2 minutes of "row sum recall" — your child is shown three numbers from a known row and asked to give the fourth.

**Hands-on construction:** Your child will build several magic squares with their own hand, including a 5×5 from scratch. Bring blank graph paper home if they ask.

**Project (Days 8–10):** Each pair designs and presents an original magic square (5×5 or larger). Three format options: poster, short demo video, or written exposition with diagrams.

## How to help

**Helpful:**
- Let your child show you the *turagagati* algorithm. Watching them teach it is the best check on understanding.
- Try the 3×3 yourself. Ask: "Why is 5 always in the centre?"
- For the project, offer to be the test audience. Ask "If I didn't know the method, would I follow your explanation?"

**Not helpful:**
- Looking up a 5×5 magic square online and showing it to them. The point is the *construction*, not the final pattern.
- Telling them it's "ancient Indian magic." It's not magic. It's a careful algorithm, and the framing matters for their relationship with mathematics.

## What success looks like

By the end of the module, your child can:
- State the magic constant formula *S = n(n² + 1)/2* and apply it for *n* = 3, 4, 5, 6, 7.
- Construct a 5×5 magic square from scratch using the stair-step method.
- Verify that a given grid is or is not magic by summing rows, columns, and diagonals.
- Place Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita's *Gaṇita-kaumudī* (1356 CE) in the chronology alongside Lo Shu and Dürer.
- Explain why the Khajuraho 4×4 square is "extra magic" — its 2×2 panels also sum to 34.

## Citation standard

Projects cite Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita, *Gaṇita-kaumudī*, Book 14 (1356 CE). The full citation chain is in the module's `sources.md`.
