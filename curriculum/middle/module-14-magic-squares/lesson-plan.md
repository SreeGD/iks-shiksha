## Pacing notes

- Days 1–2 are the conceptual core. Don't rush the *discovery* of the 3×3 — most of the lasting motivation comes from a student building one themselves.
- Day 4 introduces the *turagagati* method on the 3×3 (which students already know). This lets the algorithm be the focus, not the construction.
- Day 5 applies the same algorithm to a 5×5. If the class struggled with Day 4, push Day 5 by one day and combine it with Day 6.
- Day 6 (4×4 Khajuraho) is the cultural-context highlight. It is NOT required that students build a 4×4 themselves at Middle band — that's a Senior task. Showing the inscription and counting its panel-magic property is enough.
- Days 8–10 are project + presentation. Light enough to combine if school calendar requires.

## Daily warm-up (recurring)

Every class starts with **2 minutes of "row sum recall"**: teacher writes any 3 numbers from a known magic square; students name the 4th number that completes the row to 15. (E.g. "1, 5, ?" → 9.) Builds arithmetic fluency.

## What students leave the module with

- One personally constructed 5×5 magic square they can show a friend.
- The formula *S = n(n² + 1)/2* memorised and explained.
- An understanding that magic squares appeared independently in multiple cultures — and that Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita (1356 CE) produced the most systematic pre-modern treatment.
- A poster, demo, or short write-up as their project artefact.
