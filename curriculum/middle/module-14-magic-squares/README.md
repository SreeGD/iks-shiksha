# Module 14 — Magic Squares (*Bhadragaṇita*)

**Age band:** Middle · **Grades:** 6–8 · **Duration:** 10 sessions × 45 min

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module, students will be able to:

1. **Define** a magic square as an *n × n* grid of distinct integers whose rows, columns, and main diagonals share a common sum (the *magic constant*).
2. **Derive** the magic constant formula *S = n(n² + 1)/2* for an *n × n* square filled with the integers 1 to *n²*.
3. **Construct** any odd-order magic square (3×3, 5×5, 7×7) using Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita's stair-step (*turagagati*) method.
4. **Verify** that a given grid is or is not magic by direct row, column, and diagonal sum-checks.
5. **Recognise** the historical 4×4 *Khajuraho* magic square and explain why its panel-magic property goes beyond the basic definition.
6. **Locate** *Bhadragaṇita* (Book 14 of Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita's *Gaṇita-kaumudī*, 1356 CE) in the chronology of Indian mathematics, alongside the Chinese *Lo Shu* and Dürer's 1514 European square.

## NEP 2020 alignment

- **§4.6** — Integration of Indian Knowledge Systems: students engage Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita's algorithmic method as a piece of mainstream mathematical content, not a curiosity.
- **§4.23** — Experiential learning: discovery-by-trial of the 3×3 square, paired construction of larger squares, and a design-your-own project.
- **§11.8** — Holistic, multidisciplinary education: cross-cultural comparison (Lo Shu, Khajuraho, Dürer) and connections to combinatorics, Sudoku, and recreational mathematics.
- **§4.27** — Local context: the Khajuraho inscription is a real Indian artefact (Lakṣmaṇa temple complex, ~10th–11th c. CE) that students can locate geographically and visually.

## Prerequisites

- Confident arithmetic with single- and double-digit addition.
- Comfortable identifying rows, columns, and diagonals in a grid.
- Familiarity with the integers 1 to 25 and small multiplication facts.

## Key vocabulary

See `_shared/glossary.md` (Module 14 section).

- *Bhadragaṇita* — "mathematics of auspicious squares"; Book 14 of the *Gaṇita-kaumudī*
- *Gaṇita-kaumudī* — Nārāyaṇa Paṇḍita's 1356 CE mathematical compendium
- *samānaṅka* — equal-sum property (the magic constant)
- *turagagati* — "horse-step," Nārāyaṇa's stair-step construction method
- *yantra* — diagram (used here only to acknowledge the cultural context, not to teach ritual)

## Materials checklist

- Printed blank 3×3, 4×4, and 5×5 grids (~5 per student across the module)
- Coloured pencils or markers (3 colours per student)
- A class set of numbered tiles 1–25 (or paper squares) — at least one per pair
- Printed image of the *Khajuraho* magic square panel for Day 6
- Printed image of Dürer's *Melencolia I* (1514) — only the magic-square corner needed
- Stopwatch / wall clock for Day 5 timed construction
