# Parent Guide — Module 15: Project Implementation (Capstone)

**Your child's age:** Middle (Grades 6–8) · **Module length:** 10 sessions × 45 min over ~2 weeks

## What this module is

This is the capstone of the Indian Knowledge Systems curriculum. Across Modules 1–14 your child has met a range of frameworks — the five elements (*pañcabhūta*), Ayurvedic *doṣa*, dot-method arithmetic, moon-phase *tithi*, yoga *āsana*, herb identification, the movement of the sun, ecology, and Vedic mathematics. In Module 15 they pick **one project** that applies one of those frameworks to a real question, and they run it through a structured 10-day cycle: scope → research → design → build → document → share.

This is **not a new content module**. It is a *project sprint*. The new learning is how to run an inquiry from start to finish — set a question, find evidence, iterate, source, present.

## The project menu

Your child picks one of these. The teacher has the final say on which projects are offered to the class.

1. **Fermented-foods comparative study** — comparing curd, paneer, and a fermented batter. (This is the school-safe form of the Pañcagavya theme; see safety note below.)
2. **Mandala / *pṛthvī* geometry design** — a hand-drawn mandala using dot-method techniques.
3. **Tithi + saṁkrānti + festival calendar** — a calendar combining moon-phases, sun-positions, and the family's festival schedule.
4. **Herb identification card-deck** — photograph + drawing + description of 10 herbs from your neighbourhood.
5. **Magic-square pattern art** — a valid magic square turned into a piece of visual art.
6. **Yoga sequence design** — a 10-minute sequence designed for a chosen age group, with explanations.
7. **Sustainability audit of the school** — measure water / waste / energy in one area; propose three doable changes.
8. **Doṣa daily-rhythm self-study** — a 7-day journal of what they noticed about their own routine. (NOT a diagnosis.)
9. **Multiplication-method comparison** — timed comparison of traditional column multiplication vs Vedic methods.
10. **Open-design project** — student-proposed, anchored to a prior module, with teacher approval.

## Important safety note — Pañcagavya

You may have heard that Pañcagavya is a traditional Ayurvedic preparation using five cow-derived substances, including cow urine and cow dung. **We do not ask students to handle, prepare, taste, or ingest any form of pañcagavya in school.** Cow-urine ingestion has known safety concerns and is not appropriate for school students; raw cow dung carries hygiene risk.

The school-appropriate version of this theme is the **fermented-foods comparative study** (Project 1), which carries the same intellectual lesson (traditional Indian food technology; microbial transformation; Ayurvedic dietetics) safely.

If your child is curious about pañcagavya itself, they may do a library-only research project on it with written parental consent — no handling, balanced sources, written report only.

## The 10-day arc

| Days | What's happening |
|------|------------------|
| 1–2 | Choose the project; write a one-page plan |
| 3–4 | Research — drawing on sources from prior modules |
| 5 | Mid-project critique with a partner |
| 6–7 | Build the artefact |
| 8 | Write up the process |
| 9 | Rehearse the share |
| 10 | Project share day — small audience |

## How to help

**Helpful:**
- Be the audience for an early rehearsal — at home, on Day 9 evening. Listen without correcting.
- Help locate materials they ask for, but don't go beyond what they asked for. The project is theirs.
- Ask "what's the next thing you're doing?" rather than "is it done?" The first question keeps them moving; the second creates pressure.
- For homework: read their daily journal entry (1–5 lines). Don't grade it. Just read.

**Not helpful:**
- Doing the build. The struggle is the learning.
- Insisting the project be "impressive." A small, finished, honest project beats a large, half-finished one.
- Pushing them to defend "ancient Indian wisdom." The rubric explicitly rewards intellectual honesty — including acknowledging the limits of any framework.

## On the Day 10 share

Day 10 is a small public share with peers and a few invited parents. **It is not a performance.** Students are expected to say what didn't work, alongside what did. Applaud both.

You may be invited. Please come if you can — it matters to your child. Three to five parents in the audience is plenty.

## Time commitment at home

Across the 2 weeks:

- Daily journal — 5 min/day, written at school (no home task)
- Research / materials gathering — ~30 min total at home across Days 2–4
- Build at home — only if they couldn't finish in Day 6 / 7's class time. Cap at 30 min and stop.
- Rehearsal — 10 min on Day 9 evening (in front of you or a sibling)

Total home commitment: ~2 hours across the 2 weeks. If it's much more than that, the project is over-scoped — talk to the teacher.

## Citation discipline

Every project must cite at least one source — a verse from a prior module's source cache, a reference from a school science textbook, or a primary observation your child made themselves (with date and location). **No invented citations. No "the ancient text says…" without naming the text.** This is a habit we've built since Module 2 and it carries here.

## Assessment

| Component | Weight |
|-----------|--------|
| Project artefact | 60% |
| Documentation + journal | 20% |
| Presentation + Q&A | 15% |
| Self + peer reflection | 5% |

Total 40 marks.

## What success looks like

By end of module, your child can:

- Run a small inquiry from a question to a finished artefact.
- Cite real sources, including a primary observation they made themselves.
- Stand in front of a small audience and say *what didn't work* alongside what did.
- Write a 10-day journal that shows how they got there.

## A note on framing

This curriculum frames *pañcagavya*, *doṣa*, *pañcabhūta*, and other IKS concepts as **historical Indian frameworks for organising observation** — not as modern medical advice, and not as substitutes for chemistry, physics, or biology. The Day 10 reflection asks your child to be honest about what their chosen framework captures well and what it doesn't. That honesty is the point, and it is the mark of a serious student of any tradition.
