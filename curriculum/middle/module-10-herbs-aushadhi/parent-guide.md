# Parent Guide — Module 10: Auṣadhi (Herbs)

**Your child's age:** Middle (Grades 6–8) · **Module length:** 10 sessions × 45 min over ~2 weeks

## What your child is learning

The Ayurvedic concept of *auṣadhi* — herbs and medicinal plants — applied to six common kitchen and garden herbs:

| Sanskrit | Common name | Botanical name |
|----------|-------------|------------------|
| *tulasī* | Holy basil / tulsi | *Ocimum tenuiflorum* |
| *nimba* | Neem | *Azadirachta indica* |
| *haridrā* | Turmeric / haldi | *Curcuma longa* |
| *adraka* | Ginger / adrak | *Zingiber officinale* |
| *yavānī* | Ajwain / carom | *Trachyspermum ammi* |
| *pudīnā* | Mint / pudina | *Mentha* species |

For each herb, students learn:
- How to identify it by leaf shape and smell
- The main active compound modern chemistry has identified (curcumin, gingerol, eugenol, etc.)
- The traditional use within the Ayurvedic doṣa framework (heating vs. cooling)
- The safety question: how much is too much

## How we teach it

The framing is **observational botany + modern chemistry + cultural knowledge**, in that order. We start with what your child can see and smell, then connect to the chemistry textbook, then situate the Sanskrit framework as one historical Indian way of organising the knowledge.

We do **not** teach this instead of modern medicine. We teach it as a complementary intellectual framework — and as a way to think more carefully about plants in your child's everyday food.

NEP 2020 §4.6 (Integration of Indian Knowledge Systems) supports this approach.

## The most important takeaway

> **Food amounts are safe. Therapeutic doses are a question for trained practitioners — not for students.**

Your child will hear this every class. It is the single most important point of the module. If your child comes home and starts giving family members herbal advice, gently redirect:

- "What's a culinary amount?" (a pinch, a slice, a leaf) — safe.
- "What's a therapeutic dose?" (capsules, oils, multi-gram daily) — needs a doctor.

## Specific safety topics

We explicitly cover:

1. **Neem hepatotoxicity** — neem in concentrated form (especially neem oil) can damage the liver. We do not let students chew neem leaves in class.
2. **Essential oils are not for ingestion** at this age level.
3. **Pregnancy and children** — herb dosing differs significantly. This is a clinical question, not a homework question.
4. **"Natural" is not the same as "safe."** Poison ivy is natural. Dose matters more than origin.

If you use Ayurvedic preparations at home, this module will probably make your child more curious — and slightly more questioning. That's intentional.

## Homework you'll see

- Smell-and-identify tasks (find this herb in your kitchen; bring a leaf if you have one).
- Drawing each herb's leaf shape.
- A short kitchen-garden seed-sowing activity (tulsi or mint — both grow easily on a sunny balcony).
- A project — a 6-card herb identification deck.
- 4 writing prompts (mix of reflection, explanatory, argumentative).

## How to help

**Helpful:**
- Walk your child through your spice rack. Identify each herb together.
- If you have a balcony or yard, plant a tulsi or mint cutting with your child.
- Tell stories — "your grandmother used to make X with ginger when..." — but flag them as family stories, not as universal medical advice.
- Read the safety message in this guide with your child.

**Not helpful:**
- Encouraging your child to *prescribe* herbs to relatives. They are not trained for this and this module does not teach prescription.
- Mocking either Ayurveda or modern medicine. We teach them as complementary, not in opposition.
- Pushing capsule-form herbs on your child. The whole-plant culinary use is what's safe; the concentrated extracts are a different question.

## Citation standard

By Middle band, projects must engage with at least one source. For this module, the historical source is the *Auṣadhi-sūkta* (Ṛgveda 10.97) — a real Vedic hymn addressing plants as healing beings — cited at the level of "from this hymn" rather than as a verbatim quote your child needs to memorise. The modern compound names (curcumin, gingerol, eugenol, thymol, menthol, azadirachtin) are chemistry-textbook basics.

This trains the habit: real sources from real texts and real chemistry, not invented references.

## What success looks like

By end of module, your child can:
- Identify all six herbs by sight and smell.
- Name the main active compound in each.
- Distinguish food-amount safe use from therapeutic use.
- Read a wellness claim ("turmeric cures arthritis!") and ask: what's the evidence, what's the dose, what's the source?
- Explain why ginger is "heating" and mint is "cooling" in the Ayurvedic framework, and what that means at a basic chemical level.

## On respect across traditions

Some families treat the tulsi plant as sacred. Others use Ayurvedic remedies routinely; others not at all. The classroom frame is *both/and*: tulsi is a plant with specific chemistry AND a plant with cultural significance in many Indian households. We hold both without privileging either.

## Want to go deeper?

The Senior band (Grades 9–12) version of this module includes:
- The Auṣadhi-sūkta in IAST with translation.
- Chemical structures of curcumin, gingerol, eugenol.
- Comparative ethnobotany (how does Traditional Chinese Medicine treat ginger? How does European herbal tradition treat mint?)
- Critical evaluation of common claims using actual evidence categories.
