## Pacing notes

- Days 2–7 are one herb per day. If you only have 6 sessions total, drop Days 7 and 9 (combine mint into the Day 8 doṣa lens; rush the project into Day 10 itself).
- Day 4 and Day 5 both have a short food-preparation demo. **Check school policy on cooking-in-class before the module starts.** If not allowed, both demos can be replaced with a smell-only station — and the "golden milk" / "ginger tea" become take-home recipes.
- Activity 02 (sort by *vīrya*) needs the doṣa vocabulary from Module 4. If your students haven't taken Module 4, allocate 10 min on Day 8 to introduce *uṣṇa / śīta / vāta / pitta / kapha* before the sort.

## Suggested running theme

Open each class with the same prompt on the board: **"Which herb did you smell or eat at home since our last class?"** Two students share for 30 seconds each. By Day 10 most students will have actively noticed the herbs already living in their kitchens.

## Pacing for the safety reminder

The "**these are not prescriptions**" line gets repeated **at the start of every herb-day lesson** (Days 2–7). Yes, every one. It is the most important takeaway of the module. Students should be able to recite it back by Day 5.

## Formative vs. summative weight

- Formative quizzes (Day 3 Part A; Day 5 Part B) — **not graded**. Use them to decide who needs the pre-class refresher on Day 6.
- Summative quiz (Day 10) is closed-book, 20 min, 25 marks. Combined with the project rubric (15 marks), the module total is 40 marks.

## What students leave the module with

- The ability to identify six common herbs by sight + smell.
- One project artefact (the 6-card herb deck) that they can take home or leave for the classroom library.
- The habit of asking *"what's in this?"* and *"is it safe in this amount?"* before believing a wellness claim.
- The understanding that traditional knowledge and modern phytochemistry are **complementary**, not competing.
