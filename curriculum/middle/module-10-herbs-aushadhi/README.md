# Module 10 — Auṣadhi (Herbs)

**Age band:** Middle · **Grades:** 6–8 · **Duration:** 10 sessions × 45 min (≈ 2 weeks)

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module, students will be able to:

1. **Identify** six common South Asian herbs — *tulasī*, *nimba* (neem), *haridrā* (turmeric), *adraka* (ginger), *yavānī* (ajwain), *pudīnā* (mint) — by leaf shape, smell, and where appropriate the crushed-leaf scent.
2. **Match** each herb to its Sanskrit name, its botanical name, and the principal phytochemical compound modern chemistry has identified in it (eugenol, azadirachtin, curcumin, gingerol, thymol, menthol).
3. **Distinguish** culinary use (everyday, safe in food amounts) from therapeutic dosing (a clinical question for trained practitioners).
4. **Explain** how the *doṣa* framework from Module 4 sorts herbs into heating and cooling — *uṣṇa* and *śīta* — and predict which herb would traditionally be used for which kind of imbalance.
5. **Critically evaluate** a common claim about an Ayurvedic herb against evidence — articulating what is well-supported, what is preliminary, and what is folk-belief.

## Prerequisites

- **Helpful (not strictly required):** Module 4 — Doṣas. The heating/cooling discussion on Day 8 lands faster if students already know *vāta*, *pitta*, *kapha*.
- Class 6 / 7 biology: parts of a plant; the basic idea that plants make chemicals.
- No Sanskrit knowledge assumed.

## Key vocabulary

See `_shared/glossary.md`. New for this module:

- *auṣadhi* — herb / medicinal plant
- *tulasī, nimba, haridrā, adraka, yavānī, pudīnā* — the six herbs we'll meet
- *tikta, kaṭu* — bitter, pungent (two of the six tastes; from Module 4)
- *uṣṇa, śīta* — heating, cooling (used to classify herbs)

## Safety framing — read before teaching

This module teaches students to **recognise** herbs and to understand how they are used. It does **not** teach prescription, dosing, or therapeutic use. The repeated message throughout is:

> "These plants are safe in the small amounts people put in food. They are not safe in concentrated medicinal doses without a doctor's supervision."

Specific cautions covered in the lessons:

- **Neem** is intensely bitter and has documented liver toxicity (hepatotoxicity) at high or concentrated doses. Smell only. Do **not** chew the leaves in class. Do **not** ingest neem oil ever.
- **Essential oils** are not for ingestion at any age covered in this curriculum.
- **Pregnancy, children, ongoing medication** — dosing differs significantly and is a clinical question. Reinforce that this is not what we are teaching.
- **Allergy check** — before any smell or taste activity, ask the class about plant allergies and asthma triggers. Students with asthma may need to skip crush-and-sniff steps.

## Materials checklist (whole module)

- **Live plant samples (ideal):** tulsi plant in a pot, fresh ginger root, fresh turmeric root (or powder if root unavailable), ajwain seeds (and a small ajwain leaf cutting if accessible), fresh mint sprig, dried neem leaves (do not let students chew).
- **Backup:** clear glass jars of each as dried sample with a printed label.
- A4 paper for the herb identification card-deck (Day 9 project)
- Crayons / colour pencils for leaf-shape sketching
- Small sticky labels
- Index cards (~60) for the matching activity
- Optional: a kitchen-garden seed packet (tulsi, mint, coriander) for the homework / take-home

## Citation sources used

- ***Auṣadhi-sūkta*** — Ṛgveda 10.97 — the early Vedic hymn addressing herbs as healing beings. Referenced by sūkta number in Day 1; quoted as a short paraphrase in Day 8.
- ***Caraka Saṁhitā***, *Sūtrasthāna* — the foundational Ayurvedic text where the *doṣa*–herb framework is laid out. Referenced at the chapter level only.
- Modern phytochemistry — referred to generically ("modern reviews indicate..."). The compound names (eugenol, curcumin, gingerol, thymol, menthol, azadirachtin) are basic chemistry-textbook level and not made up.

See `sources.md` for full detail.
