## Modern phytochemistry references

These compound names are basic chemistry-textbook level. We have **not** cited specific journal papers because we were unable to verify exact titles against the RAG. Treat the following as orienting reading for the teacher:

- **Curcumin** and turmeric — broadly reviewed in journals such as *Foods*, *Nutrients*, and the *Journal of Ethnopharmacology*. Review papers note its low bioavailability when consumed alone and improved absorption when paired with piperine (black pepper).
- **Gingerol** and **shogaol** — the active compounds in fresh and dried ginger respectively. Fresh ginger contains [6]-gingerol; drying converts much of it to [6]-shogaol, which is more pungent.
- **Eugenol** — the principal volatile aromatic in *Ocimum tenuiflorum* (tulsi) and also in clove (*Syzygium aromaticum*).
- **Thymol** — the principal volatile in ajwain seed; shared with thyme (*Thymus vulgaris*).
- **Menthol** — the principal volatile in *Mentha* species.
- **Azadirachtin** — the principal limonoid in neem; well known in agriculture as a natural insect repellent.

## Modern Indian regulatory sources (teacher reference only)

- **The Ayurvedic Pharmacopoeia of India** (API), published by the Ministry of AYUSH, Government of India. Real source. Provides monographs on standard Ayurvedic herbs including all six in this module.
- **Indian Council of Medical Research (ICMR)** — periodic reviews on Ayurvedic herbs and integrative medicine.

## Sensitivity note on sources

The Ṛgveda is widely available as a religious text and as a historical primary source. We use it here as the latter — a window into how a society 3000+ years ago thought about plants and healing. We do not require students to relate to it devotionally and do not present it as devotional content in class.
