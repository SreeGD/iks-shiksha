# Teacher Notes — Module 10 Auṣadhi (Middle)

## Posture for this module

Plants make chemicals. Many of those chemicals have effects on the human body. People in many cultures have observed those effects over long timescales and built systems of use. Some of those uses are well-supported by modern pharmacology; some are still being tested; some are folk-belief.

Teach all three honestly. The framing for this module is **observational botany meets modern chemistry meets cultural memory** — and the most important habit you can leave students with is the habit of asking *"is this safe in this amount?"* before reaching for any herb-based remedy.

If a student says *"my grandmother says turmeric milk cures everything"* — that's a starting point, not a wrong answer. Acknowledge the lineage, ask what specifically is being claimed, then ask what evidence we'd want to evaluate the claim.

## The one safety message (repeat daily)

> **Food amounts: safe.** A pinch of turmeric in your daal, a leaf of mint in your raita, a slice of ginger in tea — these are how millions of people have safely used these herbs for centuries.
>
> **Therapeutic doses: a clinical question.** Powders, capsules, oils, extracts, or "take 3 tsp twice a day" — that is *medicine*, and medicine needs a trained practitioner. We do **not** teach prescription in this module.

Read this aloud on Day 1 and at least once on every herb-day (2 through 7). If you only have time for one thing on the safety side, choose this.

## Specific safety points

1. **Neem.** Do **not** let students chew the leaves. The bitterness is real and unpleasant, and concentrated neem (oil, extracts, large quantities of leaves) is **hepatotoxic** in well-documented case reports. Smell only. The seed-derived neem oil is *agricultural* — for pest control. It is **not** for ingestion.
2. **Essential oils.** Period. We do not put essential oils into bodies in this module. Not on skin, not in mouths, not in noses. They are highly concentrated and not appropriate for any school activity.
3. **Pregnancy.** If a student mentions a family member who is pregnant and considering an herb, redirect: "That's a question for the doctor or a qualified practitioner — not for the class." Some herbs studied here (notably neem oil, and some preparations of ginger at high doses) have known pregnancy contraindications. Don't speculate.
4. **Allergy check.** Before Day 2, send a parent note asking about plant allergies. Even tulsi (Lamiaceae) can trigger reactions in students with severe pollen or aromatic-plant allergies.
5. **Asthma triggers.** Crushed-leaf smell tests may bother asthmatic students. Offer them a viewing-only role; don't single them out.
6. **Hot drinks.** Ginger tea and golden milk should be lukewarm when students sip. Pre-cooled.

## Common misconceptions to watch for

1. **"Natural = safe."** This is the single most common error. Poison ivy is natural. Cobra venom is natural. The relevant question is *"safe in what amount, for whom, in what preparation?"* — not whether a substance came from a plant.
2. **"If a little is good, more is better."** No. Many plant compounds have **non-monotonic dose responses** — small amounts are beneficial, larger amounts toxic. Curcumin at culinary doses is well-tolerated by most people; at multi-gram daily doses it can cause stomach upset and, in rare cases, liver issues.
3. **"Modern science has proved Ayurveda works."** Modern science has produced evidence for some specific compounds (curcumin's anti-inflammatory mechanism is well-studied) — but "Ayurveda" as a whole is a system of practice that includes diagnosis, individual constitution, lifestyle. You cannot validate or invalidate the whole system with one molecule.
4. **"Modern science has proved Ayurveda doesn't work."** Same misconception in reverse. Absence of a positive trial for one preparation does not invalidate centuries of empirical use.
5. **"Ayurvedic herbs are interchangeable with their named modern molecule."** Whole-plant preparations contain hundreds of compounds. Isolating "the active ingredient" sometimes loses or gains effects compared to the whole.
6. **Confusing fresh and dried ginger.** *Adraka* (fresh) and *sūnṭhī* (dried) are the same plant, but Ayurveda treats them differently. Modern chemistry agrees: gingerol (fresh) and shogaol (dried) have different properties.

## What "evidence-based" means at Middle band

At Middle band, distinguish three levels of claim:

1. **Folk use** — "my grandmother gives ginger tea for upset stomach." Real human practice, not yet evidence-based in the trial sense.
2. **Mechanism evidence** — "gingerol activates TRP receptors and stimulates digestive enzyme release." Lab-level evidence; useful for explaining mechanism but doesn't directly establish clinical effect.
3. **Clinical evidence** — "in a randomised trial of 200 people, ginger reduced nausea more than placebo." Trial-level evidence.

For most herbs in this module, you'll have folk use ✓, some mechanism evidence ✓, and **limited or mixed clinical evidence**. Be honest about the gap. The honesty itself is the teaching.

## Differentiation hints

- **Tier up (fast finishers):** Have them research a **seventh** herb not covered in the module — *aśvagandhā* (Withania somnifera), *brāhmī* (Bacopa monnieri), or *triphalā* (the three-fruit combination) — and add a 7th card to their deck. They must apply the same evidence-rigor: traditional use, modern compound, safety, evidence level.
- **Tier down (scaffolding):** Reduce to four herbs (tulsi, ginger, turmeric, mint) — the most commonly encountered in Indian kitchens. Add neem and ajwain as bonus cards if time permits.

## Sensitivity notes

- **Religious framing.** Tulsi is the only herb in this module with strong devotional associations in some households. Acknowledge respectfully: "many homes treat the tulsi plant as sacred — we are studying it here as a plant with specific chemical properties; both framings can coexist."
- **Health claims at home.** Students may come in with strong claims from older relatives. Treat the relative respectfully; engage with the specific claim rather than the source.
- **Caste-and-Ayurveda discussion.** Don't open this at Middle band. If a student raises it, acknowledge it briefly ("yes, like many ancient knowledge systems, Ayurveda was historically transmitted through specific social channels — this is one reason we study it now in a school open to all") and move on.

Compress:
- Day 1 = Day 1 (intro)
- Day 2 = Days 2 + 3 (tulsi + neem)
- Day 3 = Days 4 + 5 (turmeric + ginger)
- Day 4 = Days 6 + 7 (ajwain + mint)
- Day 5 = Days 8 + 9 (doṣa lens + start deck)

Drop the "smell-test challenge" if compressed; keep the safety message.
