# Day 4 — The Four Upavedas

**Module:** What is IKS? (Middle) · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

Students name the four Upavedas and one specific contribution of each.

## Materials

- Whiteboard
- Visual cards or images (a surgical instrument, a vīṇā, a martial pose, a manuscript)

## Vocabulary

- *Upaveda* — "subordinate Veda"; applied sciences
- *cikitsā* — "treatment, therapy"

## Flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

Daily prompt + recall: name 3 of the 6 Vedāṅgas.

### Core (20 min)

1. **The Four Upavedas — the APPLIED sciences:**

   | # | Sanskrit | Domain | Primary text |
   |---|----------|--------|----------------|
   | 1 | *Āyurveda* | Medicine | Caraka Saṃhitā (~2nd c. BCE), Suśruta Saṃhitā |
   | 2 | *Dhanurveda* | Martial arts & warfare | Various manuals; partial extant |
   | 3 | *Gandharvaveda* | Music | Bharata's *Nāṭyaśāstra* (~2nd c. BCE) |
   | 4 | *Arthaśāstra* | Governance & economics | Kauṭilya's *Arthaśāstra* (~3rd c. BCE) |

   (Note: classifications vary. *Sthāpatya-veda* — architecture — sometimes counted as a 5th. Stick to the standard 4 for clarity.)

2. **Tour each briefly:**

   **Āyurveda** — *Caraka Saṃhitā* lays out a full theory of health, the three doshas (vāta, pitta, kapha), diagnostic methods, and pharmacology. Module 3 covers this in depth.

   **Dhanurveda** — From *dhanus* (bow). Military training, weapon usage, fortification. Many texts lost; what survives is referenced in epics and later manuals.

   **Gandharvaveda** — Music and performing arts. Bharata's *Nāṭyaśāstra* covers theatre, music, dance — the foundational text for Indian classical performing arts.

   **Arthaśāstra** — Kauṭilya (a.k.a. Cāṇakya) wrote a treatise on statecraft, economics, and governance. Compared sometimes to Machiavelli's *Prince*, but ~1800 years older and more systematic.

3. **A specific Upaveda achievement to remember:**
   - **Suśruta's surgery** (~6th c. BCE or earlier): the *Suśruta Saṃhitā* describes 300+ surgical procedures including cataract surgery and rhinoplasty (plastic surgery on the nose). The "Sushruta nose" reconstruction technique was learned by European surgeons via translation in the 18th century.

### Activity (15 min) — Match an Upaveda to a problem

Teacher gives 5 modern problems on the board. Students decide which Upaveda's tradition would have addressed each:

1. "I have a chronic skin issue, what would the tradition suggest?" (Āyurveda)
2. "Our city needs a new defence strategy" (Dhanurveda + Arthaśāstra)
3. "How should taxes be calculated for the rainy season?" (Arthaśāstra)
4. "I want to learn a new rāga on the vīṇā" (Gandharvaveda)
5. "Our king wants to build a court of judges" (Arthaśāstra)

Discuss: where would Suśruta have fit? (Āyurveda — surgery is a subdomain of medicine in this scheme.)

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** Read the first chapter of the *Nāṭyaśāstra* in translation. What does it claim is the *origin* of theatre?
- **Tier down:** Memorise just the four Upavedas with one-line domains.

## Teacher notes

- The classification of Upavedas is somewhat traditional and not perfectly clean. Some sources include 5 (adding Sthāpatya-veda — architecture). Stick to 4 for clarity; mention the 5th briefly.
- The Sushruta-nose-reconstruction history is genuine; European surgeons did learn the technique via 18th-century Bengali practitioners. It's a documented case of pre-modern Indian medical knowledge influencing modern medicine.
