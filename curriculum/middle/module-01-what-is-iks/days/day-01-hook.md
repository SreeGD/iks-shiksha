# Day 1 — The Hook: What Does "Indian Knowledge" Mean?

**Module:** What is IKS? (Middle) · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

By the end of this lesson, students will give an open-ended definition of "Indian Knowledge Systems" that mentions at least three distinct domains (e.g. mathematics, medicine, philosophy).

## Materials

- Whiteboard
- Sticky notes (3 per student)
- Wall poster: "Indian contributions you've encountered THIS WEEK" (to be filled together)

## Vocabulary

- *vidyā* — "knowledge, learning"
- *śāstra* — "treatise; systematic body of knowledge"
- *jñāna* — "knowledge as understanding" (different shade than vidyā)

## Flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

Question on board: *"What's one Indian-origin idea you've encountered this week, outside this class?"*

Students write on sticky notes. Post on the wall. Read aloud the variety: yoga, zero (in math), turmeric (kitchen), an Indian-named star (Rigel? actually Arabic — but the question is fertile), namaste at the start of a class, an Ayurvedic ingredient on a shampoo bottle.

### Core (20 min) — A working definition

1. **The question:** *"If someone said to you 'tell me about Indian knowledge,' what would you say?"*
   - Take 3–4 student answers. Don't correct. Note variety.

2. **Build a working definition** on the board:

   > **Indian Knowledge Systems (IKS)** = the intellectual traditions developed on the Indian subcontinent over ~3000 years — including science, mathematics, astronomy, linguistics, philosophy, medicine, music, architecture, and more.

3. **Three things IKS is NOT:**
   - **NOT only religion.** (Some IKS is religious; much is not.)
   - **NOT magical thinking dressed up as science.** (Real claims, real methods.)
   - **NOT a competitor to modern science.** (Both can be true at the same time.)

4. **Three things IKS IS:**
   - A real body of intellectual work with specific authors, dates, and texts.
   - Sometimes overlapping with, sometimes distinct from, religious tradition.
   - Worth studying as history of human inquiry.

5. **One specific example to anchor today:**
   - The Indian invention of **zero** as a number (not just placeholder) is documented in Bakhshali manuscript (~7th c. CE), and used in Bhāskara II's *Līlāvatī* (12th c. CE). It travelled via Arab mathematicians to Europe by ~12th c.
   - That's a real, dated, sourceable contribution.

### Activity (15 min) — Inventory

Each student writes on 3 sticky notes:
1. One thing they ALREADY KNEW about IKS (before today).
2. One thing they WANT TO LEARN about IKS in this module.
3. One thing they're SKEPTICAL of (any claim about IKS they doubt).

Post on the wall. Cluster by theme during break.

### Wrap-up (5 min)

- Note out loud: "By Day 10 you'll each give a 5-minute presentation on ONE specific Indian contribution. Start thinking about which strand interests you most."
- Preview Day 2: *"Tomorrow — the Vedas. Not the religious part you might have heard about. The intellectual part."*

## Student-facing content

> **What is IKS?**
>
> Indian Knowledge Systems = the intellectual traditions of the Indian subcontinent (~3000 years).
>
> Includes: mathematics, astronomy, medicine, linguistics, philosophy, music, architecture.
>
> One example: **the number zero**. First documented as a number (not just a placeholder) in the **Bakhshali manuscript** (~7th century CE). Used in Bhāskara II's *Līlāvatī* (1150 CE). Reached Europe by the 12th century via Arab mathematicians.

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** Find ONE thing you previously believed about IKS that turned out to be wrong (or oversold). Bring tomorrow.
- **Tier down:** Just remember the working definition: *intellectual traditions of the Indian subcontinent*.

## Teacher notes

- The "what would you say?" prompt often reveals real diversity — some students will say "religion," some "yoga," some "math." All are partly right.
- The "NOT only religion" framing is important early. Many students conflate IKS with Hinduism. They overlap; they are not identical.
