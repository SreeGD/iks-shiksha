# Day 6 — Indian Mathematics

**Module:** What is IKS? (Middle) · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

Students name 3 specific Indian mathematical contributions with dates and articulate why each matters.

## Materials

- Whiteboard
- Printed extract: 1 verse from Bhāskara II's *Līlāvatī* (12th c. CE) in translation

## Vocabulary

- *gaṇita* — "calculation, mathematics"
- *śūnya* — "zero, void"
- *aṅka* — "digit"

## Flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

Daily prompt + recap: 2 students share one math fact they remember about India from prior knowledge.

### Core (25 min)

1. **Three specific contributions to remember:**

   **(a) Zero (śūnya) as a number** — Bakhshali manuscript (~7th c. CE — older fragments possibly earlier). Distinct from "zero as placeholder" (which existed earlier in Babylonian and Maya systems). The Indian innovation: zero participates in arithmetic operations (you can add, subtract, multiply, divide... with zero).

   **(b) Decimal place-value system** — by 6th c. CE the Indian numeral system (with positional values for ones, tens, hundreds, etc.) was in widespread use. Adopted by Arab mathematicians (Al-Khwārizmī, ~825 CE), reached Europe by ~12th c. via Fibonacci (1202 CE).

   **(c) Infinite series (centuries before Newton)** — Mādhava of Saṅgamagrāma (~14th c. CE, Kerala school) computed infinite series for π, sine, cosine. Specifically:
   $$\pi = 4 \left(1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{7} + \dots\right)$$
   (This is now called the "Leibniz series" but Mādhava had it 300 years earlier.)

2. **A named text — Bhāskara II's *Līlāvatī* (1150 CE).** A textbook on arithmetic and algebra written for his daughter (the name "Līlāvatī" is reportedly hers). Includes problems on geometry, sequences, equations, mensuration. Read one short problem aloud (in translation).

3. **What modern math doesn't credit Indian sources for (but should):**
   - The number zero as a number (not just placeholder).
   - The "Leibniz" series for π (Mādhava had it).
   - Several trigonometric identities.

4. **What Indian math DIDN'T do:**
   - Did not develop full coordinate geometry (that's Descartes, 17th c. European).
   - Did not develop calculus formally (though Mādhava came very close with the series).
   - Did not develop modern algebraic notation.

   Both lists matter for honesty.

### Activity (10 min) — Compute the Mādhava series

Class together. On the board:
- Start at 1.
- Subtract 1/3. Add 1/5. Subtract 1/7. Add 1/9. ...
- After 4 terms: 1 − 0.333 + 0.2 − 0.143 = 0.724
- Multiply by 4: 2.896 (close to π = 3.14)
- After 10 terms it gets closer.

The series converges SLOWLY — that's one reason it wasn't useful as a calculation method. But proving the *form* was a huge mathematical achievement.

## Homework

- Compute 10 terms of the Mādhava series for π. How close do you get?

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** Read about the Madhava-Leibniz attribution debate. Why is the European name still standard?
- **Tier down:** Just remember the three contributions + their approximate dates.

## Teacher notes

- The Leibniz-Mādhava attribution is a recurring honest-credit issue. Mainstream math history is increasingly correcting this; some textbooks now use "Madhava-Leibniz series."
- Bhāskara II's *Līlāvatī* problems are charming — many are presented as little stories. If time permits, read one or two more.
