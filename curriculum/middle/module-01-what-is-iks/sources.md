# Sources — Module 1 (Middle)

## Primary sources

- **Ṛg Veda, Maṇḍala 1.1** (the Agni hymn) — opening of the oldest layer of Vedic literature. Standard translation: Jamison & Brereton (2014), *The Rigveda*. Oxford.
- **Aryabhaṭīya** (499 CE) by Aryabhata. — Sanskrit astronomical text containing the rotation-of-earth claim and pi approximation. Translation: Walter Eugene Clark (1930).
- **Sūrya-siddhānta** (early CE). — Astronomical text with planetary calculations. Translation: Burgess (1860).
- **Caraka Saṃhitā** (~2nd c. BCE) — foundational Ayurvedic text. Standard translation: P. V. Sharma (1981).

## Secondary scholarly sources

- **Plofker, K. (2009).** *Mathematics in India*. Princeton University Press. — Standard modern reference.
- **Hayashi, T. (1995).** *The Bakhshali Manuscript: An Ancient Indian Mathematical Treatise*. Egbert Forsten. — On the earliest known Indian arithmetical text (~7th c. CE).
- **Pollock, S. (2006).** *The Language of the Gods in the World of Men*. University of California Press. — On Sanskrit's role across South and Southeast Asia.
- **Wujastyk, D. (2003).** *The Roots of Ayurveda*. Penguin. — Accessible introduction.

## Comparative sources

- **Lloyd, G. E. R., & Sivin, N. (2002).** *The Way and the Word: Science and Medicine in Early China and Greece*. Yale. — Useful comparative work for Senior-band extension.

## What to tell students about sources

A strong project draws on at least one primary source (or a translation of one), at least one secondary scholarly source, and engages a counter-argument or limitation fairly. Wikipedia is acceptable for orientation but never as a primary citation.
