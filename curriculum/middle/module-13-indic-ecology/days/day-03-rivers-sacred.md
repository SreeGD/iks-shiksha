# Day 3 — Rivers as Sacred: Protection by Reverence

**Module:** Indic Ecology · **Band:** Middle · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

Students explain how treating a river as sacred functioned (sometimes well, sometimes poorly) as a method of protection in the Indic tradition, name the seven traditionally sacred rivers, and locate the nearest river to their own home.

## Materials

- Map of India, paper or projected
- Printed handout: the *sapta-sindhu* invocation (a short stanza traditionally chanted at bathing) with translation
- Two photographs: a clean stretch of a sacred river (upper Yamunā in Yamunotri, or the Gaṅgā at Devprayag) and a polluted stretch (Yamunā at Delhi, Gaṅgā at Kanpur)
- Notebook

## Vocabulary

- *sindhu* (IAST: sindhu) — "river, ocean." Also the name of the Indus river system.
- *sapta-sindhu* (IAST: sapta-sindhu; lit. "seven rivers") — a traditional list of seven sacred rivers.
- *snāna* (IAST: snāna) — "bath, ritual washing."
- *tīrtha* (IAST: tīrtha; lit. "ford, crossing") — a sacred site, often on a river.

## Lesson flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

- Show the two photographs side by side — same river, different stretches.
- Ask: *"Same religion. Same river. Same name. Why does one look sacred and the other look like a sewer?"*
- Take 2–3 student responses. Don't resolve yet.

### Core (20 min)

1. **The seven rivers.** Write on the board:

   > *Gaṅgā · Yamunā · Sarasvatī · Sindhu · Godāvarī · Narmadā · Kāverī*

   The traditional invocation (read, don't memorise):

   > "*gaṅge ca yamune caiva godāvari sarasvati /
   > narmade sindho kāveri jale 'smin sannidhiṁ kuru //*"
   >
   > "O Gaṅgā, Yamunā, Godāvarī, Sarasvatī, Narmadā, Sindhu, Kāverī — be present in this water."

   The verse is chanted while pouring even a glass of water before drinking, in many households. The idea: every drop of water carries the dignity of every great river.

2. **What "sacred" did in practice.** When a river is sacred:
   - You don't urinate or defecate in it (taboo).
   - You don't dump bodies (well — you cremate, then immerse ash, with rules).
   - You don't drain it for one farmer's field at the cost of the village.
   - You build steps (*ghāṭ*) to it; you maintain those steps.

   When a river is *not* sacred — or when the sacredness gets only ritual attention and not behavioural attention — you get the second photograph.

3. **Where the system worked and where it broke.** Sacred-status protected upper-stretch rivers and small streams in many villages for centuries. It did *not* protect the urban stretches once cities grew, industry came, and population grew faster than the river could absorb. The Gaṅgā at Haridwar (upper) is still relatively clean; at Kanpur (urban industrial) it is among India's most polluted stretches.

   This is the honest pattern: the tradition gave us a *protective attitude*, not a *technical solution*. Both are needed.

4. **The contemporary state — quick numbers.** (Source: Central Pollution Control Board, government of India, annual river-water-quality reports.) Of India's major river stretches monitored, more than 100 are in the "polluted" category (Class C or worse). Sewage is the primary cause. Industrial effluent is the second.

   The remedy is being attempted — Namami Gange (the national clean-up programme) is one example. The tradition can supply the *will* (this river is sacred — we must clean it); the engineers supply the *means* (sewage treatment plants, monitoring, accountability).

### Activity (15 min) — Find Your River

- Each student writes:
  1. **Name** of the nearest river / lake / large drain to your home.
  2. **One observation** of its current state (clean? polluted? trickle? full?).
  3. **One specific source of pollution** if you have noticed any (a particular drain, a particular factory, a particular dump).
  4. **Is it considered sacred locally?** (Yes / no / don't know.)
- Pairs share. Two pairs are picked to share with the class.
- The teacher catalogues the rivers / lakes that come up; this list is used on Day 9 for the walk planning.

### Wrap-up (5 min)

- **Formative quiz on a slip of paper (5 questions, 3 min)** — see `quizzes/formative.md` Part A.
- Collect for grading.

## Student-facing content

> **The seven sacred rivers** — *Gaṅgā, Yamunā, Sarasvatī, Sindhu, Godāvarī, Narmadā, Kāverī*.
>
> The invocation chanted before bathing or pouring water:
>
> > "O Gaṅgā, Yamunā, Godāvarī, Sarasvatī, Narmadā, Sindhu, Kāverī — be present in this water."
>
> In Indic thought, sacred = protected. When a river is sacred, you do not pollute it, drain it, or treat it as private property.
>
> **But** sacred-status alone has not stopped industrial pollution in the urban stretches. The tradition supplies the *attitude of care*; modern science and policy must supply the *engineering and the accountability*. Both are needed.

## Homework

- Find out one thing about your nearest river (or water body) that you didn't know yesterday. Examples: the river's name, where it starts, where it joins another river, whether it is monitored. Write 3 sentences.

## Differentiation

- **One tier up:** The Sarasvatī in the *sapta-sindhu* list is a "lost river" — the riverbed has been mapped by satellite (Ghaggar-Hakra system) but the river no longer flows. Look up one source on this and bring it tomorrow. What does it mean to call a *dry riverbed* sacred?
- **One tier down:** Memorise just three river names — Gaṅgā, Yamunā, Kāverī (north, north, south). The other four can come over the week.

## Teacher notes

- Some students will challenge the "sacred = protected" claim with the Gaṅgā being so polluted. Affirm the challenge. The lesson is precisely that ritual reverence without behavioural follow-through is insufficient. The tradition gave a strong starting point; we have not lived up to it.
- Avoid politicising the Namami Gange programme. Focus on it as one effort, with whatever results it has had.
- Some classes will have students whose families have done *snāna* at a sacred river. Invite them to share if they want; do not single them out. The lesson is enriched by lived experience.

## Citation(s) used in this lesson

- *sapta-sindhu* invocation — a traditional prayer chanted across many Hindu households; the exact text is found in standard prayer manuals (e.g. *Nitya Karma Pūjā Prakāśa*). Not from a single canonical scripture; treated here as a traditional liturgical verse.
- Central Pollution Control Board (Government of India) annual reports on polluted river stretches — paraphrased, not cited verbatim.
