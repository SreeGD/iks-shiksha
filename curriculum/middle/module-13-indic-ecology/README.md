# Module 13 — Indic Ecology (Vasudhaiva Kuṭumbakam)

**Age band:** Middle · **Grades:** 6–8 · **Duration:** 10 sessions × 45 min (≈ 2 weeks)

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module, students will be able to:

1. **Recall** the phrase *vasudhaiva kuṭumbakam* ("the world is one family"), its source in the Mahā Upaniṣad, and the broader half-verse it sits inside.
2. **Explain** five pillars of Indic ecological thought — *pañcabhūta* interconnection, rivers as sacred and protected, sacred groves as community biodiversity reserves, *yajña* as ecological cycle, and *ahiṁsā* extended to non-human life.
3. **Read** translations of three short anchor texts — Bhagavad Gītā 3.10–14, Atharva Veda 12.1 (Pṛthivī Sūkta selections), and Īśa Upaniṣad mantra 1 — and identify the ecological idea each one carries.
4. **Distinguish** between spiritual statements ("the Earth is our mother") and empirical claims (about biodiversity, climate, soil), and explain why both kinds of statement can be true on their own terms.
5. **Apply** the framework to one local environmental observation — a river, a sacred or unsacred grove, a polluted lot, a community garden — and design one realistic action.

## Prerequisites

- Module 2 (Pañcabhūta) completed, or equivalent comfort with the five-element framework.
- Basic familiarity with the water cycle, food chains, and what "biodiversity" means (Grade 6 science).
- No Sanskrit knowledge assumed; all terms introduced with IAST + meaning.

## Key vocabulary

See `_shared/glossary.md` for full entries.

- *vasudhaiva kuṭumbakam* — "the world / earth-itself is one family"
- *yajña* — "sacrifice, offering, reciprocal giving"
- *ahiṁsā* — "non-harm, non-injury"
- *devarakāḍu* / *sarna* — community-protected sacred grove (Karnataka / tribal Eastern India)
- *Pṛthivī* — earth, also the goddess Earth (from Module 2: *pṛthvī*)
- *sthāvara* / *jaṅgama* — immobile (plants, rocks) / mobile (animals, humans) life

## Project brief (one-line teaser)

> Each pair picks one local environmental observation — a river bank, a tree on the school campus, a plastic-heavy lot, an over-watered garden, a quiet corner — and produces a poster, a 2-minute audio piece, or a short action plan that names **(a)** which Indic ecological principle applies, **(b)** what they observed, and **(c)** one realistic action they can lead.

## Caveats this module names out loud

- Pre-modern India was not uniformly eco-friendly. Forests were cleared, soils were exhausted, and rulers built capitals on rivers that they later left depleted. Some texts contain pro-environment ethics; some practices were destructive. Both are real.
- "The Earth is our mother" is a spiritual statement. It is not a scientific claim about climate or biodiversity. Both kinds of statement can be true on their own terms; conflating them helps neither.
- The climate crisis is the work of the coming decades. Old frameworks can shape *attitude* and *attention*. They cannot substitute for measured data, policy, and engineering.
