# Teacher Notes — Module 4 Doṣas (Middle)

## Posture for this module

Lead with what students can already notice in their own bodies. Some students will think faster, fidget more, dream-wander; some will move slowly, sleep deeply, hold their attention long. These observed tendencies are the data. The *doṣa* framework is one well-developed vocabulary for sorting that data into three buckets. It is **not** a personality test, not a medical diagnosis, and not destiny.

If a student asks "is this real?" — the honest answer is: the framework is a real classical Indian system that practising Ayurvedic clinicians use today. Its claims about *biological-rhythm types* are partly supported by modern chronobiology. Its claims about *biomarker-distinguishable types* are weakly supported and contested. We teach it as a lens for self-observation, not as a science of types.

## Common misconceptions to watch for

1. **"I am a Vāta."** *No.* Everyone has all three *doṣa*s. *Prakṛti* is the *proportion* a person is born with — typically dual-dominant (e.g. *vāta-pitta*) or triple-balanced (rare). Avoid letting students walk away as one-word labels.
2. **"Doṣas are humours like medieval European humours."** Surface similarity, structural difference. Galenic humours were *fluids* (blood, yellow bile, black bile, phlegm). *Doṣa*s are functional principles — patterns of behaviour in the body, not literal substances. The Senior band explores this; at Middle band a one-line contrast is enough.
3. **"Pitta = anger, Vāta = anxiety, Kapha = laziness."** Pop simplifications. The *doṣa*s are physiological first. The emotional layer is a downstream correlation, not the definition.
4. **"Doṣas decide what you should eat."** Only loosely. Modern nutritional needs (protein, micronutrients, calories) come first. *Doṣa*-aware adjustment is a small overlay, not a substitute.
5. **Confusing *prakṛti* (Ayurveda) with *prakṛti* (Sāṅkhya).** Same Sanskrit word, different referent. In Sāṅkhya, *prakṛti* is the universal substance from which everything emerges. In Ayurveda — what we use in this module — *prakṛti* is *your* individual constitutional baseline. Mention the dual use once and move on.

## On Sanskrit pronunciation

Drill these in week 1:

- *vāta* — "VAH-tuh" (long ā)
- *pitta* — "PIT-tuh" (geminate t)
- *kapha* — "KUH-pha" (aspirated ph — like "uphill," not English "f")
- *prakṛti* — "PRUH-kri-ti" (vowel ṛ rolled lightly)
- *vikṛti* — "VI-kri-ti"
- *doṣa* — "DOE-shuh"

Common error: students will say "PITTA" with hard English t, like "pizza." The correct sound is closer to the t in Italian *spaghetti*.

## Safety scope (CRITICAL — read before Day 7)

This module trains the framework, not its clinical application. Specifically:

- **No herbal recommendations.** If a student asks "what should I take for X?", redirect: "that's a doctor's job, not ours."
- **No self-diagnosis.** Even the self-observation worksheet (Day 3) is framed as "tendency" not "type."
- **Existing care.** A student already on prescribed medication should change *nothing* based on this module.
- **Mental health.** Don't let "Vāta imbalance" become a euphemism for anxiety that a student needs help with. Refer to school counsellor.

## On the modern-science framing

The closest robust modern parallel to the *doṣa* day-clock (Day 5) is **chronobiology** — the science of circadian and ultradian rhythms in metabolism, alertness, hormone release. Cite Roenneberg's *chronotype* work if you want a name. The looser correlate is *somatotype* (Sheldon's ectomorph / mesomorph / endomorph), but Sheldon's work has serious methodological problems and shouldn't be used as a defence of *doṣa* typology. Better not to mention Sheldon at all.

The Prasher et al. (2008) genome-expression study is occasionally cited by Ayurveda-defenders. It found modest differences in self-identified *vāta/pitta/kapha* types. Replication has been mixed. At Middle band, this is too inside-baseball — leave it to Senior.

## Differentiation hints

- **Tier up (fast finishers):** Have them research one modern study on chronobiology and explain the parallel — or critique it — in one paragraph.
- **Tier down (scaffolding):** Reduce vocabulary to three terms: *vāta, pitta, kapha*. Drop *prakṛti/vikṛti* until Day 4.

## Sensitivity notes

- Some students come from families that already use *doṣa* talk casually ("don't eat that, it'll raise your pitta"). Honour this without privileging it. The class frame is: we're learning the framework carefully.
- A student with a chronic condition (asthma, diabetes, ADHD, depression) may feel exposed by the self-observation worksheet. Allow opt-out without explanation. Don't make the worksheet collection public.
- Body-image concerns: kapha is often described with words like "heavy" and "stable build." For students who already feel self-conscious about weight, be careful to frame *kapha* as a *strength* (stamina, immunity, calm) — which the classical texts do too.

## Project oversight (Days 8–10)

- Confirm pairs/individual by end of Day 5.
- Day 8: every student leaves with a *5-day plan outline* and at least one *doṣa* the plan is targeting.
- Day 9: every student leaves with a *complete* (if rough) plan and the chosen presentation format.
- Day 10: 3-minute presentations, strict timer.

## Assessment grading sequence

1. Day 3 formative quiz — mark within 24 hours; identify students needing scaffolding.
2. Day 5 formative quiz — same.
3. Day 10 summative — mark over the weekend. Combine with project rubric for final grade.
