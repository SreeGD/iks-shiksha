# Parent Guide — Module 4: Doṣas (Vāta, Pitta, Kapha)

**Your child's age:** Middle (Grades 6–8) · **Module length:** 10 sessions × 45 min over ~2 weeks

## What your child is learning

A classical Indian framework for organising body functions into three categories:

| Sanskrit | English meaning | What it governs |
|----------|------------------|------------------|
| *vāta* | "wind" — motion | Movement, breath, circulation, nervous system |
| *pitta* | "that which heats" — transformation | Digestion, metabolism, body temperature, vision |
| *kapha* | "phlegm" — structure | Mass, lubrication, immunity, stability |

Plus two important pair-concepts:

- ***prakṛti*** — the *baseline* mix of the three a person is born with.
- ***vikṛti*** — the *current* state, which can be out of balance temporarily.

The framework comes from the *Caraka Saṁhitā* (c. 1st millennium BCE/CE), one of the founding texts of Āyurveda.

## How we teach it

Three things to know:

1. **Observational, not diagnostic.** Your child is learning to notice. They are NOT learning to diagnose themselves or anyone else.
2. **Lens, not identity.** The class never tells a student "you are a Vāta." Everyone has all three *doṣa*s. *Prakṛti* is a *proportion*, not a label.
3. **Honest about evidence.** We teach where the framework matches modern chronobiology (e.g. why digestion is strongest at midday) and where modern science doesn't support its more specific claims.

NEP 2020 §4.6 (Integration of Indian Knowledge Systems) and §11.8 (multidisciplinary education) support this approach.

## Important safety note

**This module makes no medical recommendations.**

- No herbal advice. No supplements. No fasting protocols.
- No suggestion that your child change any existing medical care.
- If your child reports any actual symptom — please see a doctor, not the *doṣa* poster.

The framework is a vocabulary for noticing patterns. It is not a substitute for clinical care.

## Homework you'll see

- Days 1–3: brief self-observation notes ("when did you feel most alert today?").
- Days 4–5: small written exercises on the qualities of each *doṣa*.
- Day 6: a six-tastes home tasting (involves common foods — jaggery, lemon, salt, etc.). **Check for allergies.**
- Days 8–9: project — design a 5-day "*doṣa*-aware week."

## How to help

**Helpful:**
- Quiz your child gently on the three Sanskrit terms. They should know them by Day 4.
- Talk over your family's mealtime and sleep habits. Don't impose change — just notice patterns together.
- For the project: provide poster paper / a phone to film with. Let them write their own plan.

**Not helpful:**
- Telling your child "you're definitely a Pitta type." The classroom frame is explicitly *not* that.
- Insisting that classical Ayurveda is "scientifically proven." It's a real framework with real history; some parts have modest modern support; some don't. The honest framing is what we teach.
- Using *doṣa* language to discourage food choices your child enjoys. This module is not a diet plan.

## What success looks like

By end of module, your child can:
- Name all three *doṣa*s in IAST with their constituent elements.
- Describe at least three qualities of each *doṣa* in plain English.
- Explain the difference between *prakṛti* and *vikṛti*.
- Identify the daily *doṣa* cycle (kapha morning, pitta midday, vāta evening).
- Articulate when *doṣa* language is being used carelessly (e.g. as a fixed identity) vs carefully (as observation).
- Present a 5-day *doṣa*-aware plan with reasoning.

## On respect across traditions

Many Indian families use *doṣa* language casually at home. That's fine. The classroom adds a careful framing — primary-source citation, evidence-honest comparison with chronobiology, and a firm boundary against diagnosis. Both registers can coexist.
