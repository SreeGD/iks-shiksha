# Activity 03 — Scope Debate: Lifestyle or Doctor?

**Module:** Doṣas · **Band:** Middle · **Day:** 7 · **Time:** 15 min (within Day 7's 45 min)

## Type

Paired discussion, structured scenarios

## Goal

Students decide whether each scenario belongs in "lifestyle range — observe + adjust" or "medical territory — see a doctor," practising the safety boundary in their own words.

## Materials per class

- 10 scenario cards (template below)
- 2 large signs: **LIFESTYLE** and **MEDICAL**
- Class divides into 5 pairs (or however many pairs fit your class)

## Set-up (teacher, before class)

- Print and cut 10 scenario cards.
- Pin the two large signs on opposite walls.
- Pairs gather mid-room; cards are face-down in a stack.

## Instructions (student-facing — printable handout)

> **Scope Debate** — work in pairs. 5 scenario rounds.
>
> 1. Each pair draws a card. Read it together.
> 2. Discuss for 60 seconds. Decide: lifestyle range or medical territory? Why?
> 3. When the teacher calls time, each pair stands closer to the wall sign matching their decision. Pairs in the middle = "we couldn't agree" or "depends."
> 4. The teacher picks 2 pairs to share their reasoning. Disagreement is welcomed.
> 5. Draw the next card.

## Scenario cards (10)

> **Card 1.** Aarav, 13, has had dry, flaky skin on his hands for 3 days during a cold-dry winter week. He's eating his usual diet and sleeping normally.

> **Card 2.** Priya, 12, has had a wet, productive cough for 3 weeks. It started after a cold and has not improved. She's tired in the afternoons.

> **Card 3.** Karan, 14, gets sharp acidity / heartburn whenever he eats spicy chili. He notices it happens only after spicy meals.

> **Card 4.** Riya, 13, has had heartburn that wakes her up at 2 am every night for the last 2 weeks. It happens whether she eats spicy food or not.

> **Card 5.** Devansh, 12, is restless and chatty after exam stress. Two weeks of exams. He says his sleep is broken some nights.

> **Card 6.** Sneha, 14, has had a panic feeling at school that lasts 45 minutes and made her unable to go to class. It has happened 3 times this month.

> **Card 7.** Aarush, 13, feels sluggish on rainy mornings during monsoon. He'd rather not wake up. He's fine by mid-morning.

> **Card 8.** Maya, 12, has been unable to wake up for school for 3 weeks. She sleeps 12+ hours and still feels exhausted. She has lost interest in sports.

> **Card 9.** Vivaan, 13, gets gassy and bloated after eating raw salad. He's noticed the pattern over 2 months. Otherwise fine.

> **Card 10.** Anika, 14, has had a sharp pain in her right lower abdomen for the last 4 hours. It started mild and is now severe. She also has a slight fever.

## Suggested teacher framing (after each round)

| Card | Likely answer | Teaching point |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | **lifestyle** | brief, mild, matches *vāta* season — try oil on hands |
| 2 | **medical** | persistent cough > 2 weeks; the *kapha* framing doesn't replace seeing a doctor |
| 3 | **lifestyle** | observable cause (spicy food), brief, predictable — eat less spicy |
| 4 | **medical** | persistent, wakes from sleep — needs evaluation |
| 5 | **lifestyle** | situational, time-limited (exams) — vāta-calming may help |
| 6 | **medical** | impairs functioning, recurrent — needs counsellor / clinician |
| 7 | **lifestyle** | mild, seasonal, brief, kapha-pattern in monsoon |
| 8 | **medical** | 3 weeks of major fatigue + loss of interest — possibly depression; needs help |
| 9 | **lifestyle** | observable pattern, mild, *vāta*-aggravation from raw food — try cooked vegetables instead |
| 10 | **medical (emergency)** | acute severe pain + fever — possible appendicitis. Doctor immediately. |

## Discussion prompts (5 min, after all rounds)

- Which card was hardest to call? Why?
- Where is the line for YOU between "I'll adjust my routine" and "I'll tell my parents I need a doctor"?
- What's the cost of being wrong in each direction? (Treating a medical issue as lifestyle = harm. Treating a lifestyle issue as medical = anxiety + cost.)

## Looks-like / sounds-like

| | Looks like | Sounds like |
|---|---|---|
| Going well | Pairs in active discussion; movement between walls | "Three days isn't long enough." / "But it's been getting worse." / "Then it tips medical." |
| Needs intervention | One partner deciding, the other agreeing without thought | "It says it's mild, so lifestyle." (Without considering duration.) |

## Safety / sensitivity

- The scenarios are deliberately not about anyone in the class. Don't ask students to share personal medical info.
- If a card prompts a student to mention their own real symptom — quietly check in after class, refer to school nurse / counsellor if needed.
- The answer key is a teaching guide, not a diagnostic key. Real medical decisions are made by clinicians, not classroom cards.

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** For each medical card, students name which kind of specialist (general physician, dermatologist, mental-health counsellor, emergency) would be the right first stop.
- **Tier down:** Focus on 5 of the 10 cards. Make sure they get the obvious-emergency one (card 10).

## Clean-up

- Cards back in the stack for next class.
- Worksheets (none today — discussion-only) stay verbal.
