# Module 11 — Multiplication of Numbers Close to a Power of 10 (Nikhilam Method)

**Age band:** Middle · **Grades:** 6–8 · **Duration:** 10 sessions × 45 min

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module, students will be able to:

1. **Recall** the Nikhilam procedure for multiplying two numbers close to the same base (10, 100, 1000): write each as deficit/excess from the base, cross-subtract or cross-add for the "left part," multiply the deficits for the "right part," and read off the answer.
2. **Execute** the method on 2-digit × 2-digit problems near 100 (both below, both above, and mixed) in under 10 seconds mentally.
3. **Extend** the same method to 3-digit × 3-digit problems near 1000, padding the right part to the correct number of digits.
4. **Explain** *why* the method works using the algebraic identity (B − a)(B − b) = B(B − a − b) + ab.
5. **Decide** when the Nikhilam method is faster than long multiplication, and when long multiplication is the better choice.

## Prerequisites

- Multiplication tables fluent up to 10 × 10.
- Standard long-multiplication algorithm at 2-digit × 2-digit.
- Comfort with negative numbers at the level of `7 − 9 = −2`.
- Familiarity with place value at hundreds and thousands.
- Module 9 (Subtraction by Complements) recommended but not required — the same Nikhilam sūtra is used.

## Key vocabulary

See `_shared/glossary.md`.

- *Nikhilam Navataścaramaṁ Daśataḥ* (निखिलं नवतश्चरमं दशतः) — "all from nine and the last from ten" — Tirthaji's 2nd sūtra; the rule for computing complements from a power of 10.
- *Ūrdhva-tiryagbhyāṁ* (ऊर्ध्व-तिर्यग्भ्याम्) — "vertically and crosswise" — Tirthaji's 3rd sūtra; the general multiplication sūtra. Compared with Nikhilam on Day 7.
- *deficit* / *excess* — how much a number falls short of (or exceeds) the base.
- *base* — a power of 10 (10, 100, 1000) the two numbers are close to.
- *sūtra* — short aphorism / formula.

## Honest framing in one paragraph

The Nikhilam-based "multiplication near a base" technique appears in Bharati Krishna Tirthaji's *Vedic Mathematics* (1965, posthumous). The mathematics is genuine, fast, and useful when both numbers are within ~5–10 of a power of 10. The *attribution* — that this came from an *Atharva-veda Pariśiṣṭa* — is contested by mainstream historians of Indian mathematics (Dani 1993, Plofker 2009); see `teacher-notes.md` for how to frame this for Middle-band students.
