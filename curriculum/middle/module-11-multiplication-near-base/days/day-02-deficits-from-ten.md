# Day 2 — The Setup: Deficits from 10 (Single-Digit × Single-Digit)

**Module:** Nikhilam Multiplication · **Band:** Middle · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

By the end of this lesson, students will instantly state the deficit of any single digit from 10, AND will solve any single-digit × single-digit multiplication where both digits are 6–9 using the Nikhilam method.

## Materials

- Class wall-poster: "Deficits from 10" — the table 1→9, 2→8, ..., 9→1. Display before class.
- Drill sheet: 20 single-digit × single-digit problems (both factors 6–9).
- Stopwatch per pair.

## Lesson flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

- **Complement-to-10 lightning round.** Teacher calls 10 single digits in 30 seconds; class chants the deficit. (7 → 3. 8 → 2. 6 → 4. 9 → 1. ...)
- Repeat with a different sequence. By the end most students should be calling out the deficit faster than they can think it.

### Core (15 min) — Nikhilam at the single-digit level

1. **Set up the template on the board** (use the vertical line):

   ```
        7 │ −3       ← 7 is 3 below 10
      × 8 │ −2       ← 8 is 2 below 10
     ────┼─────
        ? │ ?
   ```

2. **The two operations:**
   - LEFT part: cross-subtract. `7 − 2 = 5`. (Or `8 − 3 = 5`. Either cross gives the same number — that's a built-in check.)
   - RIGHT part: multiply the deficits. `3 × 2 = 6`.
   - Concatenate: **56**.

3. **Verify.** `7 × 8 = 56`. ✓

4. **Why does either cross give the same answer?** Quick proof on board:
   `7 − 2 = 7 − (10 − 8) = 7 + 8 − 10`
   `8 − 3 = 8 − (10 − 7) = 7 + 8 − 10`
   Same thing. Don't dwell — Day 7 has the full algebra.

5. **The hand-trick (optional, fun).** Hold up both hands. For `7 × 8`: fold down 2 fingers on the left hand (deficit of 7 from 10 = 3, so keep 7 fingers up; or fold down 7 − 5 = 2 on the left) and 3 on the right (deficit of 8 = 2, so fold down 2 + 3 = ... use whichever finger-mnemonic the class likes). The number of fingers DOWN × 10 = the tens; the number of fingers UP on each hand multiplied = the units. (This is a folk technique that maps to the Nikhilam algebra.) Show once, don't drill it.

6. **Worked examples in chorus:**
   - `9 × 8`: deficits 1, 2. Left: 9 − 2 = 7. Right: 1 × 2 = 2. Answer: **72**. ✓
   - `6 × 9`: deficits 4, 1. Left: 6 − 1 = 5. Right: 4 × 1 = 4. Answer: **54**. ✓
   - `6 × 7`: deficits 4, 3. Left: 6 − 3 = 3. Right: 4 × 3 = 12. Hmm — right part is two digits! Carry the 1 to the left: left = 3 + 1 = 4; right = 2. Answer: **42**. ✓

7. **Note the carry rule.** When the right part has more digits than the base has zeros (here: base 10, so right part should be 1 digit; if it's 2 digits, carry the extra). Write on board:

   > "If the right part overflows the base, carry the overflow into the left part."

### Activity (20 min) — Drill

Pair drills.

- Hand out the drill sheet — 20 problems, all single-digit × single-digit, both factors in 6–9.
- Working in pairs:
  - Partner A reads problem 1 aloud.
  - Partner B answers in under 3 seconds using Nikhilam.
  - Swap.
- 10 min of paired drill. Then 5 min of individual silent practice.

After 5 minutes of paired drills, do a class-level **timing test**: project 10 problems on the board; students answer all 10 individually in 60 seconds.

### Wrap-up (5 min)

- Quick check: chorus answers — `7 × 7`? (49.) `6 × 8`? (48.) `9 × 9`? (81.) `8 × 6`? (48.)
- Preview Day 3: *"Tomorrow — both numbers near 100. Same trick, slightly bigger."*

## Student-facing content

> **Single-digit Nikhilam (base = 10)**
>
> 1. Find each digit's **deficit from 10**.
> 2. Cross-subtract for the LEFT part.
> 3. Multiply the deficits for the RIGHT part.
> 4. Stick them together. If the RIGHT part is 2 digits, **carry** the overflow.
>
> **Examples:**
> - `7 × 8`: 7−2=5; 3×2=6 → **56**.
> - `9 × 8`: 9−2=7; 1×2=2 → **72**.
> - `6 × 7`: 6−3=3; 4×3=12 → carry 1 → 3+1=4; right=2 → **42**.
>
> The Sanskrit sūtra: ***Nikhilam Navataścaramaṁ Daśataḥ*** — "all from 9 and the last from 10."

## Homework

- Practice deficit-from-10 recall. Have a parent/sibling call out single digits 1–9; you answer the deficit. Beat 20 problems in 30 seconds.
- Solve 10 problems on the back of today's worksheet (all single-digit × single-digit, both 6–9) using Nikhilam. Self-check against your times table.

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** Try `9 × 9 × 9` (chained) or apply the method to base 20 — see if `19 × 18` works the same way with base 20. (Yes — deficits 1, 2 → left 17, right 2 → 17 × 20 + 2 = 342. ✓)
- **Tier down:** Stick to `8 × 9`, `9 × 9`, `7 × 8` for today. Memorise these specific three; we'll widen the range tomorrow.

## Teacher notes

- Some students will protest: *"But I already know 7 × 8 = 56 from my times tables!"* Honest answer: *"Yes — for single digits the times-table is faster. The Nikhilam method is here so we can use it on 2-digit numbers tomorrow, where the times-table doesn't help."*
- Watch for the carry rule on `6 × 7` and `6 × 8`. This is the most common error point.
- The "either cross gives the same" property is a built-in check students can use to catch errors. Reinforce it.
