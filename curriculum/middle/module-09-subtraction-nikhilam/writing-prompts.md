# Writing Prompts — Module 9 Nikhilam Subtraction (Middle)

Eight prompts. Useful as exit tickets, homework reflections, or project warm-ups.

## Reflection prompts

**1. (Day 1, end of class, 3 min)** Describe what it felt like to subtract `10 000 − 4567` under time pressure. Did your mind borrow? Did you count? Did you panic? Be honest — there's no wrong answer.

**2. (Day 5, after the retest, 5 min)** You re-solved the same problem with Nikhilam. Did your time change? Did the *feeling* of doing the math change? Compare the two experiences in your own words.

## Explanatory prompts

**3. (Day 3 or Day 6, homework, ~120 words)** Explain Nikhilam to a Grade 4 student who has never seen it. No more than 6 sentences. Use one specific example with verification.

**4. (Day 7, homework, ~120 words)** Explain in your own words why the same idea works in base 2 (binary) as in base 10. Use one binary example. Don't use the phrase "two's complement" without explaining what it means.

## Argumentative prompt

**5. (Day 6 or homework, 1 paragraph)** Some people claim the Nikhilam sūtra is "ancient Vedic mathematics." Others say it's a 20th-century synthesis published by Tirthaji in 1965. Pick a position and defend it in 5–7 sentences. Reference what you know.

## Creative prompts

**6. (Day 4 or Day 5, optional, 1 page)** Write a short script (50–150 words) in which a frustrated student and a confident Nikhilam user have a conversation. The frustrated student raises one good objection to the method. The confident user gives an honest answer. (No "and then they magically agreed" endings.)

**7. (Day 7 or Day 8, optional, 1 page)** Imagine the CPU inside your phone could talk. It explains how it just subtracted `7 − 3`. Write a 100–200 word monologue from the CPU's point of view, in everyday language, but accurate about what it actually does.

## Cross-disciplinary prompt

**8. (Day 7, optional, 1 paragraph)** This module touched mathematics, computer science, and Sanskrit. In 3–5 sentences, describe one *other* place in your life or studies where the same idea (rounding up to a base, adjusting by the difference) shows up.

---

## Prompt-to-band notes

- For **Primary** band, prompts 1, 3, and 6 work as drawings or oral responses.
- For **Senior** band, prompt 5 deserves a full essay treatment with citations from `sources.md`. Prompt 4 can be extended into a base-*n* general analysis.

## How to give feedback

- Prompts 1, 2: acknowledge with one sentence — no correction. Listening, not judging.
- Prompts 3, 4: respond with "the part that landed" and "the part that confused me."
- Prompt 5: use the rubric — does the writing acknowledge the other side, give a specific example, reach a clear position?
- Prompts 6, 7, 8: no grading. Read the best ones aloud (with permission).
