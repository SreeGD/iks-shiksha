# Parent Guide — Module 9: Subtraction by Complements (*Nikhilam Sūtra*)

**Your child's age:** Middle (Grades 6–8) · **Module length:** 10 sessions × 45 min

## What your child is learning

A mental-arithmetic technique called the **Nikhilam method** — a way of subtracting from round numbers (10, 100, 1000, 10 000, …) without any borrowing.

The rule, in Sanskrit, is six words:

> *Nikhilaṁ Navataścaramaṁ Daśataḥ* — "all from nine and the last from ten."

In practice: to find `1000 − 567`, your child looks at each digit of 567 and reads off:

- 9 − 5 = **4**
- 9 − 6 = **3**
- 10 − 7 = **3**

Answer: **433**. No borrowing. They check it: 567 + 433 = 1000. ✓

This generalises to any power of ten, and (with a small adjustment) to general subtractions like `8243 − 567`.

## A note on "Vedic mathematics"

Important honest framing:

- The Nikhilam method is a real, useful technique. It works exactly as advertised.
- The sūtra was published in 1965 by Bharati Krishna Tirthaji (1884–1960), Śaṅkarācārya of Govardhana Maṭha (Puri). His book *Vedic Mathematics* gives 16 sūtras and credits them to a Vedic appendix-text.
- **No other Sanskritist has located that text.** The method is real; the direct Vedic attribution is unverified.
- We teach the method enthusiastically AND are honest about its history. At Senior band your child reads the scholarly debate (Dani 1993) and forms their own view.

## What to expect this fortnight

**Daily warm-up:** 1 minute of "complement to 10" — instant recall of digit-partners that add to 10.

**Day 1:** Baseline timing. Your child solves `10 000 − 4567` using their normal method. They write down the time. Day 5 retest is on the same problem with Nikhilam; the visible speedup is the motivation.

**Days 2–4:** Find the complement (*pūraka*) of any number to 100, 1000, 10 000. Apply it to subtractions like `1000 − 567`.

**Day 6:** *Why* the rule works. The algebraic statement: `10ⁿ − A = (10ⁿ − 1) − A + 1`, where `(10ⁿ − 1)` is "all nines" and "+1" is the last-from-ten step.

**Day 7:** A surprising connection — binary computers use the same idea to subtract. It's called *two's complement*. Your child sees that the Nikhilam principle is alive inside every smartphone.

**Days 8–10:** Project work and final assessment.

## How to help

**Helpful:**

- 5-minute home drill: call out two-digit numbers; your child gives the complement to 100. (`73 → 27`. `41 → 59`. `28 → 72`.) Then 3-digit complements to 1000.
- Be the "customer" in a shopkeeper role-play. You give a 1000-rupee note for a 367-rupee item; your child gives change of 633. They explain how they got it.
- Try the method yourself. Most adults find it eye-opening.

**Not helpful:**

- Insisting that "the way I was taught (borrowing)" is the only right way. Both methods give the same answer; Nikhilam is faster when subtracting from a round base.
- Doing the project for them. Even the chart or video has to be in their handwriting / their voice.

## What success looks like

By end of module, your child can:

- Recite the Nikhilam sūtra in Sanskrit and translate it.
- Find the complement of any 3-digit number to 1000 in under 3 seconds.
- Subtract from a power of ten with no written borrowing.
- Explain *why* the method works (a one-paragraph justification using place value).
- Distinguish the method (Tirthaji's, published 1965) from a vague claim of "Vedic antiquity."

## Project options

Your child chooses one of:

1. **A shopkeeper's quick-change chart** — a one-page reference card a real shopkeeper could use to give change from 100, 500, or 1000 rupee notes instantly.
2. **A 60-second teaching video** — explaining Nikhilam to a Grade 4 student.
3. **A two's-complement explainer poster** — connecting Nikhilam to how computers subtract in binary.

Projects must cite at least one source. The standard citation is:
