## Pacing notes

- **Day 1** establishes a baseline: students time themselves on `10000 − 4567` using standard borrowing. **Day 5** retests the same problem with the Nikhilam method. The visible speedup is the module's motivation.
- **Days 2–4** are the core skill: finding complements and applying them to power-of-ten subtraction. If the class is solid by Day 3, Day 4's drill can be extended.
- **Day 6** ("Why it works") and **Day 7** (two's complement) can be reordered — some classes find the binary parallel more motivating than the algebra; in that case do Day 7 first, then loop back to algebra on Day 8 morning.
- **Days 8–9** introduce the general case and revisit speed. Day 10 is summative.

## Daily warm-up (recurring)

Every class starts with **1 minute of "complement to 10" fluency**: teacher calls out a digit; students call back the partner that makes 10. (8 → 2, 6 → 4, 9 → 1, etc.) Same warm-up as Module 5 — it is the prerequisite reflex for Nikhilam.

## What students leave the module with

- Instant recall of complements to 10, 100, and 1000 for any number under 1000.
- Confidence subtracting from a power of ten without writing a borrow.
- An algebraic statement (in their own words) of why the method works: `10ⁿ − A = (10ⁿ − 1) − A + 1`, and the digit-wise rule applies because `10ⁿ − 1` is "all nines."
- One real-world application — change-counting, deficit checks, or a binary parallel.
- One project artefact they can share with a younger student.
