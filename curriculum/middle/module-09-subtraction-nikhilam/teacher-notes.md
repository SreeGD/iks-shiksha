# Teacher Notes — Module 9 Nikhilam Subtraction (Middle)

## Posture

The win in this module is **fluency**: a student looks at `10 000 − 4567` and writes `5433` without a single borrowed digit. The Sanskrit name *Nikhilaṁ Navataścaramaṁ Daśataḥ* is the cultural anchor; the *technique* is what they take home — and it is genuinely faster than standard borrowing once practised.

Honesty about provenance: the Nikhilam *sūtra* is real and printed in Tirthaji's 1965 book. The claim that it derives from a Vedic appendix is unverified and disputed by mainstream Sanskritists. Don't oversell this as "ancient." At Middle band, name the sūtra and the date (1965), and move on. Senior students unpack the historicity in depth.

## Common misconceptions

1. **"You can't subtract a bigger digit from a smaller one — that's why we borrow."** The Nikhilam method *avoids* this entirely by subtracting each digit *from 9* (and the last from 10). 9 is always bigger than any single digit, so no borrow can ever be needed. Demonstrate this on Day 2.
2. **"The complement is the answer to the subtraction."** Only when subtracting from a clean power of ten. For `1000 − 567 = 433`, yes — 433 is the complement. For `1234 − 567`, no — you have to combine the complement with the leading digits. Day 8 fixes this.
3. **"The last digit is 'special' just because Tirthaji said so."** No — `9, 9, 9, 9` would give you `9999`, not `10 000`. The "+1" needed to make `9999 → 10 000` is exactly what "the last from ten" provides. Show this on Day 6.
4. **"This is binary two's complement."** Closely related, not identical. Two's complement is "all bits flipped, plus 1." Nikhilam is "all digits from 9, last digit from 10." Same structure, different base. Be precise on Day 7.
5. **"If the subtrahend has fewer digits than the base, just pad with zeros."** Yes — `1000 − 7` means treat 7 as `007`, complement digit-wise: `9-0, 9-0, 10-7 = 9, 9, 3 → 993`. Common student error: forget to pad. Drill this on Day 4.

## Differentiation hints

- **Tier up:** Generalise — find the complement of any number to any chosen base (not only powers of ten). E.g. complement of 38 to 50 is 12. Then subtract using the same idea.
- **Tier down:** Stick to complements to 100 until Day 4. Don't move to 1000 until the 100-complement is automatic.

## Differential pacing

- A class with strong place-value baseline can compress to 7 days by combining Days 2+3 and Days 8+9.
- A class struggling with borrowing in the first place should NOT skip Day 2. The "complement to 100" drill builds the basic pattern; everything else stacks on it.

## Sensitivity notes

- Some students are anxious about timed math. The timed activities are **personal baselines** — students race themselves, not each other. No public leaderboards.
- Avoid the framing "the West has slow borrowing, India has the fast method." Standard borrowing works fine and is itself partly of Indian origin (Brahmagupta). Both methods are useful; Nikhilam is *especially* useful for subtractions from a round base.
- The Sanskrit sūtra is printed in IAST throughout. Do not transliterate ad-hoc ("nikhilam navataschcaramam"). Use the IAST form.

## Project oversight

- Three project formats (shopkeeper's quick-change chart, 60-second teaching video, two's-complement explainer poster) each have different production challenges.
- Shopkeeper chart: confirm by Day 8 that the pair has chosen a real shop scenario (e.g. "you sold a 273-rupee item with a 1000-rupee note — how much change?") and not an arbitrary list of numbers.
- Video projects: check by Day 8 that the pair has access to a phone with a camera AND a quiet place to record.
- Two's-complement poster: this is the most challenging artefact. Allow only pairs who have engaged seriously with Day 7. Strong differentiation choice.

## Citation discipline

Citations for this module are book references (e.g. *Tirthaji, Vedic Mathematics (1965), sūtra Nikhilam*) and PDF references (e.g. *FUNDAMENTAL AND VEDIC MATHEMATICS .pdf*, Nikhilam chapter). Students should cite at least one source in their project. This is the standard for math modules in this curriculum.

## If you only have 6 days

- Day 1 (hook + baseline + complement to 10)
- Day 2 (combined: complements to 100 and 1000)
- Day 3 (subtracting from power of ten + mid-module retest)
- Day 4 (why it works — pick *either* algebra *or* two's complement, not both)
- Day 5 (one project session)
- Day 6 (final assessment, light project presentation)

## What to flag for the curriculum lead

- If most students still struggle with the *base-padding* idea by Day 5, the Module 5 "make-10" reflex was not fully established. Worth a 10-minute reteach.
- If students adopt the method but can't articulate why it works on Day 6, push the algebra harder before Day 7. Two's complement only lands cleanly when the algebra has clicked.
