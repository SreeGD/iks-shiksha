# Sources — Module 3 (Senior)

## Classical primary sources

- **Caraka Saṃhitā** (~2nd c. BCE) — *Sūtra-sthāna* chapters 1, 26 most relevant. Translation: Sharma, P. V. (1981). Chaukhambha Orientalia.
- **Suśruta Saṃhitā** — surgical procedures. Translation: Bhishagratna, K. K. (1907–1916), reprinted Chaukhambha.
- **Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya** by Vāgbhaṭa (~7th c. CE) — translation: Murthy, K. R. Srikantha (1991–95).
- **Bhagavad-gītā 6.17** — *yuktāhāra-vihārasya* — regulated lifestyle. Multiple translations.

## Modern scholarly sources

- **Wujastyk, D. (2003).** *The Roots of Ayurveda*. Penguin. — Accessible primary-source translations.
- **Wujastyk, D., & Smith, F. M., eds. (2008).** *Modern and Global Ayurveda: Pluralism and Paradigms*. SUNY Press. — Engages the modern global spread.
- **Patwardhan, B., Mutalik, G., & Tillu, G. (2015).** *Integrative Approaches for Health: Biomedical Research, Ayurveda and Yoga*. Academic Press. — Engages the modern evidence question seriously.

## Modern empirical research

- **Aggarwal, B. B., & Harikumar, K. B. (2009).** "Potential therapeutic effects of curcumin, the anti-inflammatory agent, against neurodegenerative, cardiovascular, pulmonary, metabolic, autoimmune and neoplastic diseases." *International Journal of Biochemistry & Cell Biology*, 41(1), 40–59. — Strong review of curcumin evidence.
- **Patwardhan, B. (2014).** "Bridging Ayurveda with evidence-based scientific approaches in medicine." *EPMA Journal*, 5(1), 19.
- **Panda, S. (2018).** *The Circadian Code*. Rodale. — Modern lifestyle medicine; aligns with dinacharya.
- **Streeter, C. C., et al. (2010).** "Effects of yoga versus walking on mood, anxiety, and brain GABA levels." — Yoga as adjunct intervention.

## Critical / policy sources

- **AYUSH Ministry, Government of India.** Various reports on Ayurvedic integration.
- **Indian Medical Association** statements on cross-pathy.
- **The Lancet** (occasional) on traditional medicine integration concerns.

## Citation discipline

A strong Senior paper cites at least one classical primary, one modern empirical, and one historical/scholarly source. Each cited substantively, not just name-checked. Invented citations score 0 on multiple criteria.
