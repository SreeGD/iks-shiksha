# Day 4 — Modern Evidence Base for Ayurveda

**Module:** Ayurveda (Senior) · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

Students review the modern evidence base for Ayurveda, distinguishing well-evidenced interventions from poorly-evidenced ones.

## Materials

- Printed abstract: Aggarwal & Harikumar (2009) on curcumin
- Printed extract: Panda (2018) on time-restricted eating
- Whiteboard

## Flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

Recall: Tridosha clinical vs biomedical framing distinction.

### Core (25 min)

1. **Build the evidence map** on the board:

   | Intervention | Modern evidence | Quality |
   |--------------|------------------|---------|
   | **Turmeric (curcumin) — anti-inflammatory** | Well-replicated; reviews in *Int J Biochem Cell Biol* | Strong |
   | **Time-restricted eating** (aligned with dinacharya midday-meal principle) | Panda et al. — strong evidence for metabolic benefits | Strong |
   | **Yoga + breath for HRV** | Streeter (2010), multiple studies | Moderate |
   | **Tulsi (Ocimum sanctum) adaptogenic** | Modest RCT support for stress reduction | Modest |
   | **Triphala for digestive function** | Some clinical evidence | Modest |
   | **Pulse diagnosis (nāḍī parīkṣā)** | Inter-rater agreement studies — mixed | Weak |
   | **Specific dosha-based dietary prescriptions** | Mixed; many specific claims fail RCT | Weak |
   | **Pancha-karma cleansing protocols** | Limited; mostly observational | Weak |

2. **The pattern.** Things that survive evidence scrutiny:
   - Single-compound herbs with measurable biochemical effects (turmeric, ashwagandha, tulsi).
   - Lifestyle / behavioral interventions aligned with modern lifestyle medicine (regular sleep, meal timing, exercise).
   - General yoga and breath interventions.

   Things that don't survive as well:
   - Specific dosha-type prescriptions for unrelated conditions.
   - Mechanism claims that contradict modern physiology.

3. **The honest framing:** "Ayurveda has *some* strong evidence and *some* weak evidence. The mature stance is to use the strong, study the moderate, and be skeptical of the weak."

### Activity (10 min) — Read a real abstract

Hand out the Aggarwal & Harikumar (2009) curcumin review abstract. Students read silently. Then answer:
- What conditions does it discuss curcumin for?
- What's the strength of the evidence per condition?
- What hedge words does the abstract use?

### Wrap-up (5 min)

- Preview Day 5: *"Tomorrow — mid-module + paper topic commitment."*

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** Read a full RCT — find one via Google Scholar on a specific Ayurvedic intervention. Critique.
- **Tier down:** Memorise the evidence-strength table.

## Teacher notes

- The "evidence map" is the most important takeaway. Distinguish well-evidenced from weakly-evidenced. Don't lump.
- Some students will resist the "weak evidence" verdict on parts they personally value. Validate the value; hold the evidence standard.

## Citation(s) used

- Aggarwal, B. B., & Harikumar, K. B. (2009). *Int J Biochem Cell Biol*, 41(1), 40–59.
- Panda, S. (2018). *The Circadian Code*. Rodale.
- Streeter, C. C., et al. (2010). *J Altern Complement Med*, 16(11), 1145–1152.
