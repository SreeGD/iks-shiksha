# Teacher Notes — Module 3 (Senior)

## Posture

This is a serious comparative-medicine module. The honest framing:

> **Ayurveda is a sophisticated 2000-year-old framework for lifestyle medicine, daily wellbeing, and chronic care. Modern evidence supports much of it (turmeric, sleep timing, time-restricted eating). The dosha-as-strict-biomedical-classification has mixed evidence and shouldn't be oversold.**

Three failure modes:

1. **Triumphalism** — "Ayurveda has cured cancer for 5000 years." Specific claims, please, with sources.
2. **Dismissal** — "It's all placebo." Demonstrably false for several interventions.
3. **Boosterism without evidence** — "Ayurveda is just good." This is hagiography, not scholarship.

## Hard rule — medical caveat

Always: **nothing in this module is medical advice.** Students with medical conditions continue their existing care. If anyone seems to be planning to drop a treatment because of this module, intervene immediately.

## The 8 branches (aṣṭāṅga Ayurveda)

1. *Kāya-cikitsā* — internal medicine
2. *Bāla-cikitsā* — pediatrics
3. *Graha-cikitsā* — psychiatry (literally "afflictions")
4. *Ūrdhvāṅga-cikitsā* — ENT, ophthalmology
5. *Śalya-tantra* — surgery
6. *Daṁṣṭra-cikitsā* — toxicology
7. *Jarā-cikitsā* — geriatrics, rejuvenation
8. *Vṛṣa-cikitsā* — reproductive/aphrodisiacs

## Foundational texts (the bṛhattrayī — "great three")

- **Caraka Saṃhitā** (~2nd c. BCE) — internal medicine focus
- **Suśruta Saṃhitā** (~6th c. BCE or earlier?) — surgery focus
- **Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya** by Vāgbhaṭa (~7th c. CE) — synthesis, standard textbook

## Common misconceptions

1. **"Ayurveda has no evidence base."** False. Modest but real. Turmeric (curcumin) anti-inflammatory action — well-replicated. Yoga + breath for HRV — replicated. Tulsi adaptogenic effects — modest evidence. The picture is mixed but not empty.
2. **"Ayurveda has full evidence base."** Also false. Many specific dosha-based prescriptions don't hold up to RCT scrutiny. Dosha typology has weak biomarker correlates.
3. **"Pulse diagnosis (nāḍī parīkṣā) is scientifically validated."** Mixed evidence. Skilled practitioners show some inter-rater agreement; biomarker validation is weak.

## On the integration debate (Day 7)

Three positions in current scholarly + policy literature:

- **Full integration:** Ayurveda alongside biomedicine; AYUSH ministry approach in India.
- **Selective integration:** Use Ayurvedic interventions where evidence supports; reject where it doesn't. (Patwardhan et al. approach.)
- **Separation:** Ayurveda as cultural-historical heritage; biomedicine for actual treatment. (Some Indian scientists and medical bodies, e.g. Indian Medical Association on certain points.)

All three are defensible. The skill is articulating each fairly.

## Sensitivity

This is politically loaded in India. The AYUSH ministry is a real government body. Cultural pride is real. Skepticism is real. Honour both.

## Project oversight

- Strong topics: "Turmeric (curcumin) — what does the modern evidence say about its anti-inflammatory claims?" or "Is the Tridosha typology validated by modern biomarker studies?"
- Weak topics: "Ayurveda is great" / "Ayurveda doesn't work."
