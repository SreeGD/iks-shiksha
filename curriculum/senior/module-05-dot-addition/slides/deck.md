# Important honest framing

The dot method itself is **NOT** one of the 16 sūtras.

It's a 20th-century pedagogical technique that uses the *spirit* of sūtras 7 and 8.

This honesty is the foundation of the module.

---

# Tirthaji's claim

> "The sūtras (aphorisms) of *Atharva-veda Pariśiṣṭa*..."
> — Tirthaji, *Vedic Mathematics* (1965), Preface

He claimed the 16 sūtras come from a Vedic appendix-text.

---

# The provenance puzzle

**No other scholar has ever produced the manuscript** of that *Pariśiṣṭa*.

Tirthaji died (1960) before publication. The book is posthumous (1965).

---

# S. G. Dani's 1993 critique

Three critiques:

1. **No manuscript** produced.
2. **Sanskrit style** of the sūtras is not Vedic.
3. **European parallels** (Trachtenberg, etc.) suggest convergence, not Vedic origin.

Dani is a respected mathematician at TIFR / IIT Bombay.

---

**Both possibilities are honest about the math.**

# Dot method at senior speed

Target: 5-addend × 5-digit sums in under 60 seconds.

Three skill multipliers:
- **Eye scan pattern** (jump, don't serial-scan)
- **Mental running total** (no recompute)
- **Carry insertion** (write carry as tiny digit at top of next column)

---

# Sūtra in action — Sankalana

```
33 + 9 = 33 + 10 − 1 = 42
234 + 98 = 234 + 100 − 2 = 332
53 − 9 = 53 − 10 + 1 = 44
```

The named sūtra: **Sankalana Vyavakalanābhyām** — by addition and by subtraction.

---

# Sūtra in action — Pūraṇāpūraṇābhyāṁ

```
47 + 53 = 100   (complete to base)
```

In the dot method, every dot is a "completion to 10" — that's literally this sūtra.

The named sūtra: **Pūraṇāpūraṇābhyāṁ** — by completing or non-completing.

---

# Base-n generalisation

In any base *n*:

- A dot represents *n* used up.
- Dot count = carry to next column.
- Procedure is identical.

```
Base 8: column 4, 7, 3, 6, 5
→ 25_(10) = 31_(8). Units=1, carry=3.
```

Beautiful. Generalises cleanly.
