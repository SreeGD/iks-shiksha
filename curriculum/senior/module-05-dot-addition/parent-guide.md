# Parent Guide — Module 5: Bindu Paddhati (Senior)

**Your student's age:** Senior (Grades 9–12) · **Module length:** 10 sessions × 45 min

## What your student is learning

Two equally important things:

1. **The dot method at high speed** — sub-60-second mental addition on 5-addend × 5-digit columns, plus extending the technique to base 8, base 16, and even binary.

2. **The historicity question** — was "Vedic mathematics" actually Vedic? They read Bharati Krishna Tirthaji's preface (1965) and S. G. Dani's scholarly critique (1993), then form their own informed position.

## The honest framing

Your student will learn — and articulate — these uncomfortable facts:
- The dot method is **not** found in any Vedic text.
- Tirthaji claimed his 16 sūtras came from an *Atharva-veda Pariśiṣṭa* (Vedic appendix-text) that no other scholar has ever produced.
- S. G. Dani (TIFR / IIT Bombay) argues in *Frontline* (1993) and *Resonance* (2001) that the techniques are largely 20th-century innovations parallel to similar Western mental-arithmetic systems (Trachtenberg).
- The math is **real and useful**. The Vedic-provenance claim is **contested**.

This is not anti-Indian-culture. It is honest scholarship about a specific 20th-century intellectual project. Modern postural yoga has a similar history (covered in Module 8 Senior).

## Project: choose your track

5–6 page paper. Two options:

**Math track:** Generalise the dot method to base-*n* and prove correctness via place-value reasoning. Optionally extend to multiplication (*Ūrdhva-Tiryagbhyāṁ*).

**History track:** Argue a position on the labelling debate — keep "Vedic mathematics," drop it, or retain with caveat. Or place Tirthaji's project in its post-colonial historical context.

## How to help

**Helpful:**
- Be a willing test subject for their data project if they choose that.
- Read their draft and ask: *"Which point would a skeptical reader push back on?"*
- For the history track: ask them to articulate Dani's strongest critique fairly. If they strawman Dani, push back.

**Not helpful:**
- Insisting "Tirthaji was right" or "Dani was right." The point is *informed engagement*, not picking a winner.
- Confusing this module with anti-Indian-tradition. The dot method is a real Indian intellectual achievement; we just don't pretend it's older than it is.

## On the politics

Some families teach "Vedic mathematics" with pride and may feel this honest framing diminishes that pride. The classroom answer: *cultural achievements are not less valuable for being recent*. Modern postural yoga, modern Vedic mathematics, modern Indian classical music — all real, all Indian, just younger than their names suggest.

## Assessment

- Summative quiz: 35 marks.
- Paper: 65 marks (math track and history track have different rubric weightings).
- Total: 100 marks.

## What success looks like

By end of module, your student can:
- Add 5×5-digit sums in under 60 seconds using the dot method.
- Apply the dot method in non-decimal bases (8, 16, 2).
- State and explain Tirthaji's 16 sūtras.
- Articulate Dani's three main critiques of the Vedic-provenance claim.
- Defend an informed position on the labelling debate, with citations and a steel-manned counter-argument.

## Want to go further?

After this module, suggested reading:
- Tirthaji, B. K. (1965). *Vedic Mathematics: Sixteen Mathematical Formulae from the Vedas*.
- Dani, S. G. (1993). "Myths and Reality: On Vedic Mathematics." *Frontline*.
- Plofker, K. (2009). *Mathematics in India*. (Standard history.)
- Hayashi, T. (1995). *The Bakhshali Manuscript*. (For pre-Tirthaji Indian arithmetic.)
