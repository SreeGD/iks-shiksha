# Day 4 — Empedocles, Aristotle, and the Greek Four

**Module:** Pañcabhūta (Senior) · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

Students compare the Greek (Empedocles + Aristotle) four/five element scheme with Sāṅkhya, identifying at least three structural differences and one structural similarity.

## Materials

- Printed extracts:
  - Empedocles fragments on the four roots (Kirk, Raven & Schofield translation)
  - Aristotle, *On Generation and Corruption*, II.3 (on the four elements + aither)
- Whiteboard
- Side-by-side comparison worksheet (blank)

## Flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

- Recall: the 5-bhūta + 5-tanmātra + 5-indriya structure of Sāṅkhya.

### Core (25 min)

1. **Empedocles in brief.** Read 1-paragraph paraphrase aloud:
   > Empedocles (c. 490–430 BCE) proposed four "roots" (*rhizomata*) — earth, water, air, fire. He added two forces — Love (*philia*) that brings them together and Strife (*neikos*) that separates them. The world is the cyclic combination/separation of the four under these forces.

2. **Aristotle in brief:**
   > Aristotle (4th c. BCE) inherited the four sublunary elements (earth, water, air, fire) but added a *fifth element* — *aither* (the "quintessence") — which is the substance of the heavenly spheres. The four are characterised by pairs of qualities: hot/cold and wet/dry.

   | Element | Aristotle's qualities |
   |---------|-------------------------|
   | Fire | hot + dry |
   | Air | hot + wet |
   | Water | cold + wet |
   | Earth | cold + dry |
   | Aither | none (incorruptible) |

3. **Side-by-side comparison.** Build with class on the board:

   | | Sāṅkhya (India) | Empedocles + Aristotle (Greece) |
   |---|----------------|----------------------------------|
   | Number of elements | 5 | 4 + 1 (quintessence) |
   | Origin | Emerge sequentially from *prakṛti* | Eternal "roots" |
   | What drives combination | Three *guṇas* (sattva, rajas, tamas) | Love and Strife (Empedocles); the natural motions toward "proper place" (Aristotle) |
   | Fifth element identity | *Ākāśa* — universal medium of space, present everywhere | *Aither* — celestial substance, only in the heavens |
   | Quality-count rule | 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 (predicted from emergence order) | No equivalent — each element gets a pair of qualities (hot/cold + wet/dry) |
   | Perceptual mapping (tanmātra–indriya) | Yes — explicit, symmetric, one-to-one | No equivalent |
   | Includes consciousness in scheme | Yes (puruṣa, 25th tattva) | No |

4. **Discuss the most interesting difference.** Open: *"Which of these structural differences feels most significant to you? Why?"*

### Activity (10 min)

Begin in-class comparison essay draft:
- Pick ONE structural difference from the table.
- Write a 1-paragraph (5–7 sentence) analysis of why it matters.
- This is a draft for the longer Day 8–10 paper.

### Wrap-up (5 min)

- Preview Day 5: *"Tomorrow — we read SB 3.26.36 closely in IAST, word by word."*

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** Read Empedocles fragment B17 (on Love and Strife) in translation; write 1 paragraph on whether the *guṇa* doctrine in Sāṅkhya is structurally parallel.
- **Tier down:** Focus on Aristotle alone; skip Empedocles for now.

## Teacher notes

- The asymmetry on perceptual mapping (Sāṅkhya YES, Greek NO) is the most distinctive cross-cultural point. Push students to notice it.
- Some students will be tempted to conclude "Sāṅkhya is more sophisticated." Push back: *more sophisticated in what way?* (More internal symmetry, yes. More predictive of chemical behaviour, no.)

## Citation(s) used

- Aristotle, *On Generation and Corruption*, II.3. (Any standard translation.)
