# Day 8 — Project Session 1: Outline Your Paper

**Module:** Pañcabhūta (Senior) · **Time:** 45 min

## Learning objective

Students choose a focused paper topic, draft a one-paragraph thesis, and produce a working outline.

## Materials

- Project brief (`assessments/project-brief.md`)
- Rubric (`assessments/rubric.md`)
- Laptop or notebook for drafting

## Flow

### Warm-up (5 min)

- Hand out the rubric. Read aloud the *thesis* and *counter-argument* criteria.

### Core: outlining (30 min)

**Sprint 1 (10 min) — Topic.** Each student writes:
- Working title (1 line)
- One-paragraph thesis (3–4 sentences)
- 3 sources they'll cite (must include one primary scriptural, one secondary scholarly, one comparative or modern)

Teacher rotates: 60 sec per student, single question: *"What's the counter-argument someone could make against your thesis?"*

**Sprint 2 (10 min) — Outline.** Each student writes section headings + 1-line sub-points:
- Introduction (with thesis statement)
- Section 1 (anchor claim + source)
- Section 2 (anchor claim + source)
- Counter-argument and response
- Conclusion

**Sprint 3 (10 min) — Begin drafting.** Each student starts the introduction or one body paragraph.

### Wrap-up (10 min)

- Each student exchanges outline with one buddy.
- 3-min mutual feedback: 1 strength + 1 fix.
- Outline + thesis submitted at the door.

## Homework

- Draft 1–2 more paragraphs at home. Bring everything tomorrow.

## Differentiation

- **Tier up:** Add a 4th source — a contemporary academic article (search Google Scholar).
- **Tier down:** Topic can be narrow ("a comparison of one Sāṅkhya tanmātra concept with the modern five-senses model"). Narrower is fine.

## Teacher notes

- Common pitfall: too-broad topic. Push for specificity early.
- A weak thesis sounds like "Indian science is interesting." A strong thesis sounds like "The Sāṅkhya tanmātra–indriya symmetry offers a model that 19th-century European psychophysics independently rediscovered."
