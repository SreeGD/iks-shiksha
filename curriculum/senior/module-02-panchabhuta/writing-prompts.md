# Writing Prompts — Module 2 Pañcabhūta (Senior)

Eight prompts at essay-level depth.

## Reflection prompts

**1. (Day 1)** Categories are conventions imposed by minds, not features of nature. Defend or refute in 1 page, using examples from the periodic table and the Sāṅkhya tattva enumeration.

**2. (Day 6)** Modern science excludes consciousness from chemistry. Sāṅkhya includes puruṣa as the 25th tattva. Is this an asset or a liability of the Sāṅkhya scheme? 1 page.

## Explanatory prompts

**3. (Day 3 or Day 5)** In 1 page, explain to a college-level reader why the quality-count rule (1, 2, 3, 4, 5) is a *predicted consequence* of the Sāṅkhya emergence sequence, not an arbitrary stipulation. Use SB 3.26.36 as a primary source.

**4. (Day 4)** Compare and contrast the role of Empedocles's Love-and-Strife with the role of the three guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas) in Sāṅkhya. 1 page.

## Argumentative prompts

**5. (Day 7 or Day 8 — could be paper topic)** Argue for or against the claim: "Sāṅkhya's bhūta-tanmātra-indriya symmetry is structurally more complete than Aristotle's element-quality scheme." 2 pages, with sources.

**6. (Senior research)** Argue: "The Sāṅkhya scheme's inclusion of ākāśa as a positive category (rather than as absence-of-matter) anticipates a question that modern field theory addresses but did not solve until the 20th century." 2 pages.

## Cross-disciplinary prompts

**7. (Day 6)** Pick a contemporary debate (consciousness studies, panpsychism, the hard problem of consciousness) and write 1 page on how Sāṅkhya's tattva enumeration speaks to it — or fails to.

**8. (Stretch)** Write a 2-page comparison of the *guṇa* doctrine in Sāṅkhya with the *element* doctrine in classical Chinese philosophy (wǔxíng — Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water). Use one specific source from each tradition.

---

## How to grade essays

- Strong: clear thesis, specific citations, fair counter-argument, original thinking, clean prose.
- Adequate: clear thesis, some citations, weak counter-argument, mostly competent prose.
- Weak: vague thesis, no citations, no counter-argument, structural issues.
