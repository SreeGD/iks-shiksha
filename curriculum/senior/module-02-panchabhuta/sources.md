## Secondary scholarly sources

- **Larson, G. J. (1979).** *Classical Sāṁkhya: An Interpretation of its History and Meaning*. Motilal Banarsidass. **The standard reference on Sāṅkhya tattva enumeration.** Use for the 25-tattva ladder on Day 2.
- **Plofker, K. (2009).** *Mathematics in India*. Princeton University Press. **Historical context** of classical Indian intellectual frameworks; useful for situating Sāṅkhya in time.
- **Bryant, E. F. (2009).** *The Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali*. North Point Press. — Modern annotated translation of Patañjali; useful for connecting Sāṅkhya and Yoga.

## Comparative sources (Day 4)

- **Empedocles** (c. 490–430 BCE) — Fragments on the four roots: earth, water, fire, air. English translations available in Kirk, Raven & Schofield, *The Presocratic Philosophers* (2nd ed., Cambridge 1983).
- **Aristotle** (c. 4th century BCE) — *On Generation and Corruption*, Book II. Discussion of the four sublunary elements + the celestial *aither* (quintessence).

For both Empedocles and Aristotle, free English translations are available via the Perseus Project (perseus.tufts.edu) and the Internet Classics Archive.

## Modern science context

- NCERT Class 11 Chemistry, Chapters 1–3 (atomic structure, classification of elements).
- For plasma and Bose-Einstein condensate as states of matter beyond the classical four: any modern physics survey text.

## Citation style for student papers

- For scripture: `(SB 3.26.36)` inline; full reference in works cited as `Bhāgavata Purāṇa, Canto 3, Chapter 26, Verse 36. Translation: A. C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada (1972).`
- For scholarly works: standard MLA or Chicago. State chosen style on title page.
- For comparative source: `(Empedocles, fragment B17)` or `(Aristotle, GC II.3)`.
- No fabricated citations. The teacher will check at random.

## What to tell students about the source mix

Three different *kinds* of source are used in this module:
1. **Primary scriptural** (verses themselves) — facts about what the tradition says.
2. **Secondary scholarly** (Larson, Plofker, Bryant) — academic interpretation and historical context.
3. **Comparative** (Empedocles, Aristotle, modern science) — cross-cultural and cross-period comparison.

A strong paper cites all three. A weak paper cites only one type and presents it as the whole picture.
