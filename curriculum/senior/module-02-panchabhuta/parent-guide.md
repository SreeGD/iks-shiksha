# Parent Guide — Module 2: Pañcabhūta (Senior)

**Your student's age:** Senior (Grades 9–12) · **Module length:** 10 sessions × 45 min

## What your student is learning

This is a comparative-philosophy module at undergraduate-introductory level:
- **The full 25-tattva enumeration** of Sāṅkhya philosophy (not just 5 elements).
- **Primary-source reading** — at least one Sanskrit verse (SB 3.26.36) in IAST with word-by-word gloss.
- **Cross-cultural comparison** — Sāṅkhya vs. Empedocles vs. Aristotle vs. modern science.
- **A 5-page argumentative paper** with cited primary, secondary, and comparative sources.

## What's at stake

Your student will engage these honest questions:
- Is the Sāṅkhya framework "outdated" by modern science, or addressing different questions?
- Does Sāṅkhya's inclusion of consciousness (puruṣa, the 25th tattva) anticipate the "hard problem" of modern philosophy of mind?
- Should classical Indian intellectual frameworks be integrated into mainstream science class? (The Day 7 structured debate.)

## Paper expectations

5–6 pages, double-spaced. Required:
- Clear thesis in opening paragraph.
- At least one verse from primary scripture (IAST + translation).
- At least one secondary scholarly source (Larson, Plofker, Bryant).
- At least one comparative or modern source (Empedocles, Aristotle, NCERT chemistry).
- One steel-manned counter-argument with reasoned response.
- Works-cited in MLA or Chicago format.

Plagiarism is taken seriously — invented citations score 0 on multiple criteria.

## How to help

**Helpful:**
- Be a thinking partner. Listen to their thesis. Push back gently. *"What's the strongest counter-argument someone could make?"*
- Read their draft and circle the parts that aren't clear. Don't rewrite.
- For the Day 7 debate: ask them to defend a position they DON'T hold. (This is the hardest form of argument.)

**Not helpful:**
- Writing portions of the paper for them.
- Insisting on a "correct" position on the Day 7 debate. The discipline is in articulating any position fairly, not in reaching the right answer.
- Avoiding the historical / cultural sensitivities. Talk about them honestly.

## On the politics

This module touches on contested terrain. Some families have strong views on whether IKS belongs in science class. The classroom frame:
- Both positions ("yes" and "no") can be defended.
- The skill is articulating each position on its best terms.
- Your student will form their own view through engagement, not by being told.

You may disagree with their conclusions. That's healthy. Disagree by *defending your view*, not by dismissing theirs.

## Assessment

- Summative quiz: 35 marks.
- Paper: 65 marks (heavy weight on counter-argument quality and source diversity).
- Total: 100 marks.

Grading bands: Distinction (85+), Merit (70+), Pass (55+), Resit (<55).

## What success looks like

By end of module, your student can:
- Read a Sanskrit verse with translation and discuss it.
- Compare Sāṅkhya and Aristotle's element schemes structurally, with one specific point each captures that the other doesn't.
- Write a research paper that holds multiple frameworks simultaneously without collapsing them.
- Disagree with a peer's position without strawmanning it.

## Want to go further?

After this module, suggested reading:
- Larson, G. J. (1979). *Classical Sāṁkhya: An Interpretation of its History and Meaning*.
- Plofker, K. (2009). *Mathematics in India*.
- Bryant, E. F. (2009). *The Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali*.

All available in major academic libraries.
